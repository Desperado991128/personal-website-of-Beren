Daily Concept: Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction

1. What is it?
Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction refer to the spatial and temporal characteristics of eye movements generated when users engage with AI systems or their outputs. These patterns capture where, when, and how long a person looks at specific interface elements, generated text, or AI-driven suggestions. Researchers analyze these signals to infer latent cognitive states such as attention, confusion, or trust during the collaborative process.

2. How does it work?
The core mechanism involves recording raw eye-tracking data, such as fixations and saccades, and mapping them onto the stimulus provided by the AI, often text generated by a Large Language Model (LLM). This process typically transforms raw screen coordinates into a time-series of feature vectors \( X = \{x_1, x_2, ..., x_T\} \), where each vector \( x_t \) represents attributes like fixation duration or pupil dilation aligned with a specific token. Machine learning models then process these sequences to detect anomalies or specific reading behaviors, effectively treating gaze as a supplementary signal for understanding user attention and intent.

3. Why does it matter?
This concept matters because it provides a passive, real-time window into user cognition without requiring explicit feedback like surveys or clicks. In the context of LLMs, gaze data helps evaluate model outputs by detecting confusion or cognitive load, for instance when a user re-reads a hallucinated or complex sentence. It enables the development of adaptive AI systems that dynamically adjust their responses or explanations based on the user's implicit attention signals.

4. Key insight
Gaze serves as a rich implicit feedback signal that bridges the gap between the text an LLM generates and the cognitive effort required for a human to process it. This allows AI systems to move beyond treating users as passive recipients and instead model them as active participants whose attention shapes the interaction.

Paper Summaries

SIAgent: Spatial Interaction Agent via LLM-powered Eye-Hand Motion Intent Understanding in VR

Authors: Zhimin Wang, Chenyu Gu, Feng Lu
arXiv: 2603.00522v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the high learning costs and low error tolerance associated with current VR interaction paradigms that require users to memorize specific gestures. It investigates whether an "Intent-to-Operation" framework powered by Large Language Models (LLMs) can effectively interpret natural eye-hand motions to execute tasks without predefined commands.

2. Positioning
Current VR interaction follows an "Operation-to-Intent" paradigm, forcing users to adapt their behavior to rigid system requirements. This work positions itself as a shift toward "Intent-to-Operation" interaction, filling the gap between raw spatial input and semantic understanding. It leverages advances in LLMs to bridge the semantic gap that traditionally required users to learn specific gesture vocabularies.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the SIAgent framework, which integrates LLMs to translate spatial eye-hand coordination data into natural language for intent inference. It proposes a novel pipeline that maps continuous gaze and motion signals to discrete tasks via an agent-based execution module. The work demonstrates that this approach outperforms traditional gaze-pinch methods, achieving an accuracy of \(97.2\%\) compared to \(93.1\%\) while reducing physical fatigue.

4. Methodology
The proposed system consists of an intent recognition module and an agent-based execution module. The intent recognition component translates spatial interaction data, specifically eye gaze and hand trajectories, into natural language descriptions that an LLM processes to infer user goals. Once the intent is established, the system generates an agent to execute the corresponding task in the virtual environment.

5. Limitations
The abstract does not explicitly detail technical limitations, but the reliance on LLMs suggests potential latency issues compared to rule-based gesture recognition. The evaluation was restricted to a set of over 60 specific tasks, which may not fully represent the complexity of open-ended or highly ambiguous interactions in diverse VR scenarios. Additionally, the system's accuracy depends heavily on the quality of the intermediate translation from spatial data to text prompts.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research offers a significant advancement in gaze-based interaction by utilizing LLMs to handle the semantic ambiguity of natural behavior. The reported improvements in accuracy and user preference strongly validate the "Intent-to-Operation" approach over standard gaze-plus-pinch techniques. However, the practical deployment of such a system will depend on optimizing the inference speed of the LLM to ensure real-time responsiveness in interactive VR environments.

IRIS: Intent Resolution via Inference-time Saccades for Open-Ended VQA in Large Vision-Language Models

Authors: Parsa Madinei, Srijita Karmakar, Russell Cohen Hoffing et al.
arXiv: 2602.16138v1

Summary

1. Research Question
This paper investigates how to resolve ambiguity in open-ended Visual Question Answering (VQA) using Large Vision-Language Models (VLMs). Specifically, it asks whether real-time eye-tracking data can effectively clarify user intent during inference without requiring additional model training.

2. Positioning
Existing Large Vision-Language Models often struggle with ambiguous queries where the user's intent is unclear from the image and text alone. This work bridges the gap between human intent and model understanding by leveraging implicit behavioral signals, specifically eye movements, rather than relying on explicit user clarifications or specialized model training.

3. Key Contribution
The authors introduce IRIS, a novel training-free technique that integrates real-time eye-tracking to improve VLM accuracy. They demonstrate that gaze fixations synchronized with the start of the verbal query are the most critical for disambiguation, more than doubling accuracy on ambiguous questions from 35.2% to 77.2%. Additionally, they release a new benchmark dataset and a real-time interactive evaluation protocol to facilitate future research in gaze-augmented multimodal AI.

4. Methodology
IRIS operates by capturing user gaze data in real-time during a VQA interaction. It identifies the specific fixation points that occur closest to the onset of the user's verbal question and uses this spatial data to guide the VLM's attention. This process effectively focuses the model on the relevant image region, allowing it to resolve potential ambiguities without modifying the model's underlying weights.

5. Limitations
The primary limitation is the hardware requirement, as this approach necessitates accessible and accurate eye-tracking equipment for end users. There may also be challenges regarding the synchronization precision required between the gaze capture and the verbal query onset. Furthermore, the effectiveness relies on the user actually fixating on the subject of their question, which might not always occur in natural, casual interactions.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work presents a compelling intersection of cognitive science and multimodal AI, offering a significant performance boost for ambiguous queries without the cost of fine-tuning. The finding regarding the specific timing of fixations provides valuable insight into human-computer interaction dynamics. However, the practical deployment is currently limited by the need for specialized eye-tracking hardware, though future integration with standard webcams could broaden its applicability.

Human-Centered Multimodal Fusion for Sexism Detection in Memes with Eye-Tracking, Heart Rate, and EEG Signals

Authors: Iván Arcos, Paolo Rosso, Elena Gomis-Vicent
arXiv: 2602.23862v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper investigates whether augmenting automated content analysis with human physiological signals can improve the detection of sexism in memes. It specifically asks if biological markers provide an objective measure of human perception that purely computational models miss.

### 2. Positioning. The authors position this work as a response to the limitations of content-only models that struggle with the ambiguity and plausible deniability inherent in memes. It fills a critical gap by shifting the focus from the content itself to the human reaction to that content, introducing a human centered paradigm to multimodal learning.

### 3. Key Contribution. The main contribution is the creation of a novel multimodal dataset linking meme content with Eye Tracking, Heart Rate, and EEG data. The study also demonstrates that integrating these physiological signals with Vision Language Model (VLM) features yields a statistically significant improvement in detection accuracy, particularly for nuanced categories of sexism.

### 4. Methodology. Researchers recorded physiological responses from 16 subjects viewing 3984 memes, identifying correlations between sexist content and increased cognitive load. They then proposed a fusion architecture that combines these biological inputs with textual visual embeddings derived from a VLM to perform binary and fine grained classification.

### 5. Limitations. The primary limitation is the small sample size of 16 participants, which may restrict the generalizability of the physiological patterns observed. Furthermore, the requirement for specialized hardware like EEG and eye trackers presents a significant barrier to deploying this method in real time, large scale content moderation systems.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work provides strong evidence that gaze behavior and neural activity offer valuable signals for disambiguating complex social content. While the hardware requirements limit immediate practical application, the substantial F1 score increase for difficult categories validates the hypothesis that human context is essential for detecting subtle forms of sexism.

GazeXPErT: An Expert Eye-tracking Dataset for Interpretable and Explainable AI in Oncologic FDG-PET/CT Scans

Authors: Joy T Wu, Daniel Beckmann, Sarah Miller et al.
arXiv: 2603.00162v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the critical shortage of expert readers for FDG-PET/CT scans and the lack of interpretability in current AI diagnostic tools. It investigates how capturing and integrating expert eye-tracking data can enhance the performance and explainability of AI models in oncologic imaging.

2. Positioning
Current AI solutions for lesion segmentation often function as "black boxes," creating a barrier to clinical adoption due to reliability and workflow concerns. This work positions itself at the intersection of medical imaging and human-computer interaction, providing a resource to bridge the gap between automated analysis and expert cognitive processes.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the release of GazeXPErT, a comprehensive 4D eye-tracking dataset derived from 346 FDG-PET/CT studies read by both trainees and experts. The authors provide 9,030 gaze-to-lesion trajectories formatted for machine learning, demonstrating empirically that integrating this gaze data significantly improves tumor segmentation performance and enables novel intention prediction tasks.

4. Methodology
The authors collected raw 60Hz eye-tracking data during routine clinical reads, synchronizing gaze points with PET/CT image slices to create COCO-style annotations. They validated the dataset by training a 3D nnUNet model augmented with gaze maps and a vision transformer model to predict dynamic lesion localization and expert intent.

5. Limitations
While the abstract highlights the dataset's utility, potential limitations include the inherent noise in eye-tracking hardware and the challenge of standardizing gaze patterns across different reader experience levels. Additionally, the specific focus on FDG-PET/CT may require further adaptation for the methods to generalize to other imaging modalities.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work represents a significant step forward for explainable AI in healthcare by quantifying the "expert intuition" often missing in standard training datasets. The quantitative boost in segmentation performance (DICE score improvement from 0.6008 to 0.6819) strongly validates the utility of gaze as a supervisory signal. However, the true impact will depend on the community's ability to develop models that can effectively leverage this complex spatiotemporal data in real-time clinical workflows.

EyeLayer: Integrating Human Attention Patterns into LLM-Based Code Summarization

Authors: Jiahao Zhang, Yifan Zhang, Kevin Leach et al.
arXiv: 2602.22368v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper investigates whether human eye-gaze patterns can serve as a proxy for expertise to guide and enhance Large Language Models (LLMs) in the task of code summarization. It asks if integrating human attention signals into LLMs improves the quality of generated natural language descriptions of source code.

### 2. Positioning. While LLMs have advanced code summarization significantly, current models lack explicit guidance derived from human cognitive processes during code reading. This work positions itself at the intersection of software engineering and cognitive computing, filling the gap by treating gaze data as an actionable signal for model augmentation rather than just an analytical metric.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is EyeLayer, a lightweight attention-augmentation module that seamlessly integrates human gaze data into various LLM architectures. It introduces a novel mechanism using a Multimodal Gaussian Mixture to model attention, allowing the system to transfer human attention priors across different model families without disrupting pre-trained representations.

### 4. Methodology. EyeLayer models human attention by redistributing token embeddings based on learned parameters \((\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)\) that represent the location and intensity of developer focus. This mathematical approach captures where humans look most intensively and adjusts the model's internal attention weights accordingly. The module is evaluated by fine-tuning models like LLaMA-3.2, Qwen3, and CodeBERT on datasets containing eye-tracking information.

### 5. Limitations. The primary limitation is the reliance on high-quality eye-tracking datasets, which are resource-intensive to collect and may not cover the vast diversity of programming languages and coding styles found in standard text-only corpora. Additionally, the effectiveness of the Gaussian Mixture modeling relies on the assumption that human gaze patterns are consistent and generalizable across different developers and code complexities.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling fusion of cognitive science and deep learning, demonstrated by impressive performance gains of up to 13.17% on BLEU-4 scores across diverse architectures. Its strength lies in the "plug-and-play" nature of the module, which enhances semantic focus without requiring a complete model restructure. However, the practical adoption may be hindered by the scarcity of gaze data in real-world software engineering pipelines, limiting its immediate applicability compared to standard fine-tuning approaches.
