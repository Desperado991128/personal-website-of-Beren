Daily Concept: Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction

1. What is it?
Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction refer to the distinct spatial and temporal characteristics of eye movements exhibited by users while engaging with artificial intelligence systems. These patterns serve as implicit behavioral signals that reveal underlying cognitive processes such as attention allocation, cognitive load, and trust formation. Researchers analyze these signals to understand how users comprehend and evaluate outputs generated by Large Language Models (LLMs) and other AI agents.

2. How does it work?
Eye-tracking hardware captures the point of regard coordinates \((x, y)\) at high temporal frequencies. Software pipelines segment this raw data into fixations, periods where the eye remains relatively still to process information, and saccades, rapid movements between these points of interest. In LLM research, these fixations are often aligned with specific tokens in the generated text to analyze reading behaviors. 3. Why does it matter?
This concept matters because gaze data provides a passive, real-time feedback mechanism that does not require explicit user intervention like rating or editing. By identifying where users hesitate or re-read, developers can detect hallucinations or ambiguous phrasing in LLM outputs that might not be caught by automated metrics alone. Furthermore, integrating gaze signals into reward models can align AI behavior more closely with human cognitive processing and preference.

4. Key insight
Gaze patterns act as a window into the user's mind, offering a rich signal for optimizing AI interpretability and trustworthiness beyond what text-based feedback alone can provide.

Paper Summaries

SIAgent: Spatial Interaction Agent via LLM-powered Eye-Hand Motion Intent Understanding in VR

Authors: Zhimin Wang, Chenyu Gu, Feng Lu
arXiv: 2603.00522v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the high learning costs and low error tolerance of current VR interaction paradigms that require users to memorize specific gesture-command mappings. It investigates whether an LLM-powered system can effectively shift this paradigm from "Operation-to-Intent" to "Intent-to-Operation" by understanding natural, unscripted eye-hand motions.

2. Positioning
Current literature on VR interaction relies heavily on explicit command bindings, forcing users to adapt their behaviors to rigid system requirements. This work fills a critical gap by leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) to interpret user intent semantically, allowing the system to adapt to the user's natural habits rather than requiring the user to learn machine-defined gestures.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the SIAgent framework, which eliminates the need for gesture memorization by translating spatial eye-hand tracking data into natural language for intent inference. The system demonstrates that an LLM-driven approach can achieve higher recognition accuracy (97.2%) than standard gaze-pinch methods (93.1%) while significantly reducing physical fatigue.

4. Methodology
The proposed method utilizes a two-stage pipeline where spatial interaction data is first translated into natural language descriptions of user motion. An LLM then processes these descriptions to infer the user's intent and generates an autonomous agent to execute the corresponding task within the virtual environment.

5. Limitations
While the abstract highlights high accuracy, potential limitations include the inference latency of LLMs, which could introduce lag in real-time interactive systems. Additionally, translating continuous, nuanced spatial motions into discrete natural language prompts may lead to information loss or ambiguity in highly complex scenarios.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work presents a significant advancement in multimodal interaction by successfully bridging the gap between raw tracking data and semantic understanding via LLMs. The reported improvement in usability and fatigue reduction suggests a strong potential for this technology to redefine user interfaces, though real-world viability will depend on the system's ability to minimize latency during the intent inference phase.

IRIS: Intent Resolution via Inference-time Saccades for Open-Ended VQA in Large Vision-Language Models

Authors: Parsa Madinei, Srijita Karmakar, Russell Cohen Hoffing et al.
arXiv: 2602.16138v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates how to resolve ambiguity in open-ended Visual Question Answering (VQA) for Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs). It specifically asks whether real-time eye-tracking data can serve as a training-free signal to disambiguate user intent and improve response accuracy.

2. Positioning
Existing LVLMs often fail when visual questions are ambiguous because they lack the specific context of the user's focus. This work positions itself at the intersection of cognitive science and multimodal AI, introducing gaze as an implicit input signal to bridge the intent gap. It moves beyond static image-text pairs by incorporating dynamic human attention mechanisms during inference.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is IRIS, a training-free inference method that leverages eye-tracking to identify the most relevant image regions based on user fixations. The authors demonstrate that fixations aligned with the start of a question are the most informative, boosting accuracy on ambiguous questions from \(35.2\%\) to \(77.2\%\). They also release a new benchmark dataset and an evaluation suite for gaze-based VQA.

4. Methodology
IRIS operates by monitoring user eye movements in real-time during the question-asking phase. It identifies fixations that occur closest to the onset of the verbal query and uses this spatial data to guide the model's attention. This allows the LVLM to resolve referential ambiguity without any fine-tuning or weight updates.

5. Limitations
A significant limitation is the requirement for specialized eye-tracking hardware, which may hinder widespread adoption compared to software-only solutions. The method also assumes a tight temporal coupling between gaze and speech, which might vary across different user populations or cognitive styles. Additionally, the study focuses on a specific set of ambiguous pairs, and scalability to broader VQA tasks remains to be fully explored.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research presents a novel and highly effective integration of human cognitive signals into LVLM workflows. The dramatic performance improvement on ambiguous questions highlights the value of gaze as an input modality, though the hardware dependency remains a practical hurdle. It successfully demonstrates that "looking" is a crucial component of "asking" for AI systems.

N-gram-like Language Models Predict Reading Time Best

Authors: James A. Michaelov, Roger P. Levy
arXiv: 2603.09872v1

Summary

1. Research Question
This paper investigates why state-of-the-art language models, despite their superior performance in next-word prediction, often fail to accurately predict human reading times. It asks whether reading behavior is more closely aligned with simple statistical patterns rather than the complex probabilities generated by advanced transformers.

2. Positioning
The authors address a recent paradox in psycholinguistics where improvements in language model perplexity do not necessarily translate to better correlations with eye-tracking data. The work positions itself against the trend of using increasingly complex architectures for cognitive modeling. It fills a gap by explaining why simpler models often outperform sophisticated ones in predicting human processing effort.

3. Key Contribution
The main contribution is the finding that language models which mimic simple n-gram statistics provide the best fit for reading time data. The paper demonstrates that the divergence between modern transformer outputs and human reading times arises because humans rely on shallower statistical cues. This suggests that human reading comprehension may be optimized for local, n-gram-like regularities rather than the deep contextual dependencies captured by transformers.

4. Methodology
The researchers analyzed various neural language models to measure the correlation between their output probability distributions and traditional n-gram probabilities. They then compared these correlations against the models' ability to predict eye-tracking metrics, such as fixation durations, on naturalistic text datasets. This approach isolated the "n-gram-likeness" of the models as the predictive factor for human reading behavior.

5. Limitations
The study focuses primarily on the statistical correlation between model probabilities and reading times, potentially overlooking other cognitive factors like working memory constraints or syntactic processing difficulty. The reliance on "naturalistic text" may limit the applicability of the findings to specialized or domain-specific reading tasks where complex reasoning is required. Additionally, the binary comparison between n-gram and transformer features might oversimplify the spectrum of statistical features humans utilize.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work provides a valuable corrective to the assumption that better artificial language models inherently serve as better cognitive models. It offers a compelling explanation for the "more is less" phenomenon in psychometric modeling. However, the conclusion that humans rely primarily on n-gram statistics risks understating the role of higher-level semantic integration and long-distance dependencies that are known to influence eye movements in complex sentence structures.

GazeMoE: Perception of Gaze Target with Mixture-of-Experts

Authors: Zhuangzhuang Dai, Zhongxi Lu, Vincent G. Zakka et al.
arXiv: 2603.06256v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper addresses the challenge of estimating human gaze targets from visible images in a way that generalizes well across different scenarios. It specifically asks how to effectively integrate multi-modal cues, such as eye appearance and head pose, into a pre-trained vision model to improve accuracy.

### 2. Positioning. This work positions itself at the intersection of gaze estimation and the emerging field of large vision foundation models. It fills a gap in existing literature by moving beyond simple fine-tuning, instead proposing an adaptive mechanism to handle the diverse visual cues required for precise gaze prediction.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is GazeMoE, a novel end-to-end framework that applies the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) concept to gaze estimation. It introduces a class-balancing auxiliary loss and specific data augmentation techniques to solve the common problem of imbalance between "in-frame" and "out-of-frame" gaze classifications.

### 4. Methodology. The authors utilize a frozen pre-trained vision foundation model as a backbone and attach Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) modules to selectively process gaze-related features. This is combined with strategic data augmentations, such as region-specific cropping, to enhance the model's robustness and classification performance.

### 5. Limitations. While the abstract highlights state-of-the-art results, it does not discuss the computational overhead or inference latency introduced by the Mixture-of-Experts architecture. Additionally, the reliance on specific data augmentations suggests the model may require careful calibration when deployed in environments that differ significantly from the training benchmarks.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. GazeMoE represents a significant step forward by successfully adapting the "expert" routing mechanisms common in Large Language Models (LLMs) to the domain of computer vision. The strength of this approach lies in its ability to dynamically weigh different visual cues, though the practical deployment challenges regarding model complexity remain an area for further investigation.

Thinking with Gaze: Sequential Eye-Tracking as Visual Reasoning Supervision for Medical VLMs

Authors: Yiwei Li, Zihao Wu, Yanjun Lv et al.
arXiv: 2603.06697v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. This paper investigates whether sequential eye-tracking data can serve as effective supervision to improve visual reasoning in medical Vision-Language Models (VLMs). Specifically, it asks if training models to mimic the temporal order of human gaze can enhance performance and robustness on radiology tasks.

### 2. Positioning. Current VLMs typically process images as visual tokens but rely heavily on text for intermediate reasoning, creating a disconnect in visually grounded tasks like radiology. This work positions itself at the intersection of medical AI and cognitive science, arguing that mimicking the expert behavior of radiologists (sequential visual search) is superior to standard text-only reasoning chains.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is a novel training framework that incorporates sequential gaze data through dedicated "gaze tokens" within the model architecture. This approach forces the model to explicitly predict gaze-selected image patch indices in a temporal order, effectively supervising the visual reasoning process rather than just the final textual output.

### 4. Methodology. The authors introduce a small set of specialized tokens trained to predict the location of gaze fixations on image patches over time. By optimizing these tokens to match the temporal sequence of radiologists' eye movements, the model learns to integrate visual evidence in a human-like manner before generating a diagnosis.

### 5. Limitations. The approach relies on the availability of high-quality, temporally ordered eye-tracking data, which is expensive to collect and scarce compared to standard image-text pairs. Additionally, the method assumes that mimicking human gaze trajectories is the optimal reasoning strategy for the model, which may not always be the most efficient path for machine inference.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work presents a promising direction for improving the trustworthiness of medical AI by grounding reasoning in observable human behavior. While the reliance on gaze data limits scalability, the reported state-of-the-art results and improved zero-shot robustness suggest that aligning model attention with expert visual search patterns is a highly effective regularization strategy.
