Daily Concept: Eye tracking in multimodal AI

1. What is it?
Eye tracking in multimodal AI involves integrating gaze data as a distinct input signal alongside text and images within large models. It treats human visual attention as a supervisory signal or an interactive prompt to guide model reasoning. This approach bridges the gap between passive visual perception and active human-like attention mechanisms.

2. How does it work?
The system encodes spatiotemporal gaze coordinates into feature embeddings that align with the vector space of the language model or visual encoder. These embeddings act as a conditioning signal, often modulating the attention weights to prioritize specific image regions or text tokens. For instance, a model might optimize a combined objective where the probability of a target token \( y \) is conditioned on the image \( I \), text \( T \), and gaze heatmap \( G \), expressed as \( P(y | I, T, G) \). This guides the model to focus on areas humans deem relevant during training or inference.

3. Why does it matter?
This integration significantly improves model interpretability by aligning machine attention maps with human visual saliency. It also enhances computational efficiency by enabling models to process only the most salient visual features rather than the entire input space. Such alignment is critical for developing reliable assistive technologies and diagnosing model hallucinations in complex visual tasks.

4. Key insight
Gaze serves as a direct proxy for human intent, offering a weak supervision signal that connects visual stimuli to linguistic meaning.

Paper Summaries

Rapidly deploying on-device eye tracking by distilling visual foundation models

Authors: Cheng Jiang, Jogendra Kundu, David Colmenares et al.
arXiv: 2604.02509v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. How can we rapidly deploy high-accuracy, on-device gaze estimation models for new AR/VR hardware configurations without relying on extensive labeled real-world data? The paper investigates whether visual foundation models can be effectively adapted to handle specialized near-eye infrared imagery and hardware variations.

### 2. Positioning. The authors position their work against the difficulty of retraining models for every new generation of AR/VR hardware, where camera placement and illumination often change. They identify a performance gap where off-the-shelf Visual Foundation Models (VFMs) fail to deliver high accuracy on specific near-eye infrared images despite their success on natural images. This work bridges the divide between general-purpose VFMs and the specialized, resource-constrained domain of mobile eye tracking.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is DistillGaze, a two-stage framework that distills a VFM into a lightweight model suitable for on-device deployment. The method achieves a 58.62% reduction in median gaze error compared to synthetic-only baselines while compressing the model to just 256K parameters. This provides a practical recipe for leveraging both scalable synthetic data and readily available unlabeled real data to solve regression tasks in resource-constrained environments.

### 4. Methodology. DistillGaze operates in two distinct stages. First, it adapts a large VFM into a domain-specialized teacher model using self-supervised learning that combines labeled synthetic data with unlabeled real data to bridge the domain gap. Second, it trains a compact student model for on-device use by distilling knowledge from the teacher and utilizing self-training techniques.

### 5. Limitations. While the method reduces the need for labeled real data, it still requires the collection of unlabeled real images from new devices to effectively bridge the synthetic-to-real domain gap. The performance is also inherently bounded by the quality and realism of the synthetic data generation pipeline. Additionally, the two-stage training process adds complexity compared to direct supervised learning approaches.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling solution to the "hardware drift" problem in VR/AR, where collecting labeled data for every new device prototype is impractical. The strength of the approach lies in its pragmatic use of synthetic data for supervision and unlabeled data for domain adaptation, resulting in impressive error reduction and a tiny model footprint. However, the reliance on a specific distillation pipeline may require careful tuning when applied to vastly different hardware setups or diverse populations not represented in the synthetic data.

Gaze-Regularized Vision-Language-Action Models for Robotic Manipulation

Authors: Anupam Pani, Yanchao Yang
arXiv: 2603.23202v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the difficulty current Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models face in performing fine-grained robotic manipulation tasks due to a lack of active visual attention mechanisms. It investigates whether integrating human gaze patterns as a supervisory signal can guide robot perception to improve task performance and training efficiency.

2. Positioning
This research positions itself at the intersection of cognitive science and robot learning, addressing the gap between passive visual processing and active human-like attention in VLA architectures. It distinguishes itself from prior work by offering a solution that requires no architectural changes or specialized hardware during deployment. The authors argue that human gaze provides a natural, information-dense prior for intent and planning that current models lack.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is a gaze-regularized training framework that aligns the internal attention maps of VLA transformers with human gaze heatmaps. This approach improves manipulation task performance by 4-12% and accelerates training convergence without increasing inference-time computational costs. Furthermore, the method enhances model interpretability by producing visualizations that mirror human visual strategies.

4. Methodology
The authors transform temporally aggregated human gaze heatmaps into patch-level probability distributions. They then regularize the transformer's self-attention layers by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the model's attention weights and these gaze distributions. This creates an inductive bias toward task-relevant visual features while keeping the underlying model architecture intact.

5. Limitations
While the framework requires no eye-tracking hardware during deployment, it relies on the availability of human gaze data for training, which may limit its applicability to datasets lacking such annotations. There is also a potential domain gap between human visual strategies and the optimal visual processing required for specific robot embodiments. Additionally, the paper does not deeply explore scenarios where human gaze might be misleading or suboptimal for robotic execution.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work presents a practical and efficient method for improving robot learning by leveraging human biological data as a supervisory signal. The strength of the approach lies in its "plug-and-play" nature, allowing for performance gains without the latency often associated with more complex attention mechanisms. However, the dependency on gaze-annotated training data could become a bottleneck for scaling to broader tasks compared to purely self-supervised methods.

Beyond Scanpaths: Graph-Based Gaze Simulation in Dynamic Scenes

Authors: Luke Palmer, Petar Palasek, Hazem Abdelkawy
arXiv: 2603.28319v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. How can we accurately model human attention in dynamic driving scenes without losing temporal fidelity? The core research question asks how to explicitly simulate raw gaze trajectories over time rather than relying on static saliency maps or implicit scanpath dynamics.

### 2. Positioning. Existing literature often collapses gaze data into static saliency maps or treats gaze dynamics implicitly within sequential scanpaths. This work positions itself as a shift toward explicit temporal modelling. It fills a critical gap by formulating gaze prediction as an autoregressive process that directly unrolls raw trajectories conditioned on the evolving environment.

### 3. Key Contribution. The paper introduces a unified framework that treats gaze modelling as an autoregressive dynamical system using raw, unfiltered data. It proposes the Affinity Relation Transformer (ART) and the Object Density Network (ODN) to handle complex scene interactions and stochastic gaze shifts. Additionally, the authors release the Focus100 dataset, a new resource containing raw gaze data from 30 participants viewing egocentric driving footage.

### 4. Methodology. The authors represent driving scenes as gaze-centric heterogeneous graphs where nodes encode the driver, traffic objects, and road structure. The Affinity Relation Transformer (ART) processes these graphs to model spatial-temporal interactions. The system then utilizes the Object Density Network (ODN) to predict the probability distribution of the next gaze point, generating trajectories step-by-step in an autoregressive fashion.

### 5. Limitations. The specific focus on driving scenes may limit the generalizability of the model to other dynamic environments without further fine-tuning. Training directly on raw gaze data, while beneficial for realism, introduces significant noise and jitter that the model must robustly handle. The reliance on graph construction also implies a dependency on accurate object detection and scene parsing within the pipeline.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling advancement by moving away from heuristic fixation filtering and embracing the stochastic nature of raw gaze data. The application of graph transformers to model the relational geometry of driving scenes is a methodologically sound choice for capturing context. However, the computational complexity of processing dynamic graphs could pose challenges for real-time deployment in safety-critical automotive systems.

Gaze-Regularized VLMs for Ego-Centric Behavior Understanding

Authors: Anupam Pani, Yanchao Yang
arXiv: 2603.23190v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question
The paper investigates how integrating human eye gaze data into Vision Language Models (VLMs) can improve the understanding of egocentric behavior and the prediction of future actions. It specifically asks how gaze information can be effectively incorporated into the model architecture to align machine attention with human attention patterns.

### 2. Positioning
Current methods for egocentric behavior understanding typically rely solely on visual data, neglecting the rich insights regarding human intent provided by eye movements. This work positions itself at the intersection of cognitive science and multimodal AI, filling a gap by treating gaze as a primary input signal rather than an auxiliary metric.

### 3. Key Contribution
The main contribution is a novel gaze-regularized framework that integrates gaze data directly into the VLM architecture through gaze-based queries and attention regularization. This approach results in a nearly 13% improvement in semantic scores for future event prediction compared to baseline models that do not utilize gaze data.

### 4. Methodology
The authors propose a method that generates gaze-based queries to dynamically focus the model on regions highlighted by human fixations and saccades. A gaze-regularization mechanism is applied during training to force the model internal attention maps to align with actual human attention patterns.

### 5. Limitations
The provided text does not explicitly list specific limitations. However, the approach inherently relies on the availability of high-quality, synchronized eye-tracking data, which is more resource-intensive to collect than standard egocentric video. This dependency may limit the applicability of the method to datasets where such gaze annotations are absent.

### 6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a compelling demonstration of how physiological signals can ground multimodal models in human-like processing. While the reported performance gains are significant, the practical impact depends on the scalability of gaze data collection and the robustness of the model against noisy or incomplete gaze signals.

FixationFormer: Direct Utilization of Expert Gaze Trajectories for Chest X-Ray Classification

Authors: Daniel Beckmann, Benjamin Risse
arXiv: 2603.22939v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates how to effectively integrate expert eye-tracking data into medical image analysis without losing its inherent sequential properties. Specifically, it asks whether transformer architectures can leverage raw gaze trajectories to improve chest X-ray classification accuracy compared to traditional methods that rely on static heatmaps.

2. Positioning
Previous research largely relied on CNN-based systems, which struggle to process the sequential and sparse nature of gaze data, often converting dynamic eye movements into static heatmaps. This work positions itself by identifying a natural synergy between the sequential structure of gaze trajectories and the token-based architecture of transformers. It fills a critical gap by preserving the temporal dynamics of expert attention, which earlier methods typically discarded.

3. Key Contribution
The authors introduce FixationFormer, a novel transformer-based architecture that encodes gaze trajectories as token sequences to preserve both temporal and spatial information. This approach allows for fine-grained integration of diagnostic cues through explicit cross-attention between image features and gaze tokens. The method achieves state-of-the-art classification performance on three benchmark chest X-ray datasets, demonstrating the superiority of sequential gaze modeling over reduced representations.

4. Methodology
FixationFormer represents expert fixations as a sequence of tokens and processes them jointly with image feature tokens within a transformer framework. The model employs cross-attention mechanisms to directly align the gaze sequence with relevant image regions. This design enables the system to handle the sparsity and noise typical of eye-tracking data while utilizing the temporal order of fixations as a diagnostic cue.

5. Limitations
A primary limitation is the dependency on expert gaze data for training, which is resource-intensive to collect and may not be available for all medical imaging tasks. The model's performance is also inherently bounded by the quality and consistency of the eye-tracking recordings, necessitating robust handling of inter-expert variability. Additionally, the paper does not fully explore how the model would perform in a clinical setting where real-time gaze data is unavailable during inference.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research offers a significant methodological advancement by recognizing the structural compatibility between human gaze sequences and transformer attention mechanisms. The move away from aggregated heatmaps to raw sequential data allows the model to capture the diagnostic reasoning process rather than just the final areas of interest. However, the practical deployment of such a system is limited by the scarcity of large-scale gaze datasets, potentially restricting its applicability to broader medical imaging domains.
