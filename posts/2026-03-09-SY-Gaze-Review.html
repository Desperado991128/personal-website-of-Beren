Daily Concept: Fixation detection algorithms

### 1. What is it?
Fixation detection algorithms are computational methods used to classify raw eye-tracking data into discrete states of visual attention, distinguishing between fixations where the gaze remains relatively stationary and saccades where the eye moves rapidly. In the context of gaze and LLM research, these algorithms transform noisy coordinate streams into meaningful temporal units that represent when a user is actively processing specific tokens or visual regions. This process is a fundamental preprocessing step for translating raw sensor data into inputs that machine learning models can interpret.

### 2. How does it work?
The most common approach is the Velocity-Threshold Identification (I-VT) algorithm, which calculates the point-to-point velocity of the eye and classifies samples below a specific speed threshold as fixations. Mathematically, the velocity \( v_i \) between successive gaze points \( p_i \) and \( p_{i-1} \) is computed as \( v_i = \frac{\|p_i - p_{i-1}\|}{\Delta t} \). If the velocity exceeds a predefined threshold \( \theta \), the segment is labeled as a saccade, while lower velocities indicate a fixation. Alternatively, dispersion-based algorithms identify fixations by finding clusters of points that remain within a small spatial radius for a minimum duration.

### 3. Why does it matter?
These algorithms are critical for aligning human gaze behavior with language model architectures because they filter out high-frequency noise and motor movements unrelated to cognitive processing. By isolating fixations, researchers can map specific durations of visual attention to corresponding text tokens, enabling the training of models that predict human reading patterns or simulate human-like attention mechanisms. This alignment allows LLMs to leverage gaze as a supervisory signal, improving efficiency in tasks like scanpath prediction or semantic grounding.

### 4. Key insight. Fixation detection bridges the gap between raw physiological signals and semantic understanding, turning a continuous stream of coordinates into discrete attention events. This discretization is what allows researchers to compare human visual attention directly with the token-level attention weights of Large Language Models.

Paper Summaries

GazeMoE: Perception of Gaze Target with Mixture-of-Experts

Authors: Zhuangzhuang Dai, Zhongxi Lu, Vincent G. Zakka et al.
arXiv: 2603.06256v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper addresses the challenge of developing generalizable neural architectures for estimating human gaze targets from visible images. It specifically investigates how to efficiently integrate multi-modal cues, such as eye appearance and head pose, into a robust prediction framework.

### 2. Positioning. This work builds upon recent advances in pre-trained vision foundation models, aiming to bridge the gap between general feature extraction and specific gaze estimation tasks. It positions itself as a solution to the difficulty of adaptively decoding multi-modal cues, which is a limitation in current methods that struggle to balance diverse visual signals like gestures and scene context.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is the GazeMoE framework, which introduces Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) modules to selectively leverage gaze-related cues from a frozen foundation model. Additionally, the authors propose a class-balancing auxiliary loss and specific data augmentation strategies to improve robustness against class imbalance in gaze target classification.

### 4. Methodology. The proposed architecture keeps the vision foundation model frozen while attaching Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) adapters to learn domain-specific expertise for gaze estimation. To handle the imbalance between "in-frame" and "out-of-frame" targets, the method employs a class-balancing loss alongside region-specific cropping and photometric data augmentations.

### 5. Limitations. The abstract does not explicitly list limitations, but the reliance on a frozen foundation model may restrict the adaptation of low-level visual features for niche or degraded imaging conditions. Additionally, while MoE architectures are efficient during inference, they introduce routing complexity and require careful tuning to ensure distinct expert specialization.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This paper presents a compelling application of the Mixture-of-Experts paradigm, popular in Large Language Models, to the specific domain of gaze estimation. The strategy of keeping the foundation model frozen while using experts for decoding is efficient and aligns well with current trends in parameter-efficient fine-tuning. It effectively addresses practical challenges like class imbalance, making it a robust contribution to human-robot interaction research.

Sticky-Glance: Robust Intent Recognition for Human Robot Collaboration via Single-Glance

Authors: Yuzhi Lai, Shenghai Yuan, Peizheng Li et al.
arXiv: 2603.06121v1

Summary

Here is the summary of the research paper "Sticky-Glance. Robust Intent Recognition for Human Robot Collaboration via Single-Glance".

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the challenge of achieving robust gaze-based intent recognition in complex, multi-object environments. Specifically, it asks how a system can accurately identify user intent despite common issues like gaze noise, micro-saccades, and dynamic changes in the scene.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself within the domain of assistive robotics and Human-Robot Collaboration (HRC), focusing on users with limited motor capabilities. It fills a critical gap in existing literature by addressing the fragility of current gaze-tracking methods, which often struggle to maintain accuracy in cluttered or dynamic real-world settings.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the "Sticky-Glance" algorithm, an object-centric framework that stabilizes intent by anchoring it to specific objects. The paper also introduces a continuous shared control paradigm with multi-modal feedback, which improves task efficiency and reduces user workload.

4. Methodology
The proposed method utilizes an object-centric gaze grounding framework that jointly models geometric distance and direction trends to stabilize the user's focus. This approach allows the system to "anchor" the inferred intent to a target object with as few as three gaze samples, even when the target is moving or the viewpoint changes.

5. Limitations
While the abstract demonstrates high performance, a potential limitation of the object-centric approach is its reliance on the system's ability to detect and segment objects in the environment. If the computer vision component fails to identify an object, the gaze grounding mechanism may not function correctly.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research presents a significant technical advancement by enabling robust intent recognition with minimal data samples, achieving a selection accuracy of \(0.98\) for static targets. The integration of this algorithm into a shared control loop demonstrates practical utility, offering a reliable solution for communication-impaired users and improving the fluidity of human-robot interaction.

SIAgent: Spatial Interaction Agent via LLM-powered Eye-Hand Motion Intent Understanding in VR

Authors: Zhimin Wang, Chenyu Gu, Feng Lu
arXiv: 2603.00522v1

Summary

1. Research Question
How can Virtual Reality (VR) interaction systems move beyond rigid gesture memorization to understand user intent naturally? The paper addresses the high cognitive load and low error tolerance of current "Operation-to-Intent" paradigms by investigating if an LLM-powered agent can interpret natural eye-hand motions to execute tasks.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself as a paradigm shift from traditional command-based interfaces, such as gaze-and-pinch or controller buttons, which force users to learn specific machine inputs. It fills a critical gap in the literature by introducing an "Intent-to-Operation" model where the system adapts to the user's natural habits rather than requiring the user to adapt to the system's constraints.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the SIAgent framework, which acts as a semantic bridge between raw spatial interaction data and executable actions using Large Language Models (LLMs). It introduces a novel pipeline that translates eye-tracking and hand-motion data into natural language descriptions, allowing the system to infer intent without predefined gesture libraries. This approach demonstrates that intent-driven design can outperform traditional methods in accuracy (97.2% vs 93.1%) and usability.

4. Methodology
The proposed method utilizes a two-stage pipeline involving intent recognition and agent-based execution. It captures eye-tracking coordinates and hand motion trajectories, translates these spatial inputs into natural language prompts, and feeds them into an LLM to deduce the user's goal. Once the intent is established, the system generates an agent to autonomously execute the corresponding virtual task, removing the need for precise manual control.

5. Limitations
While the abstract highlights high accuracy, potential limitations include the latency inherent in LLM inference, which could hinder real-time responsiveness in fast-paced VR scenarios. Additionally, the system's reliance on translating spatial data to text may occasionally lose subtle motion nuances, and the scope of the 60 tested tasks may not fully represent the complexity of highly dynamic VR environments.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research offers a compelling application of LLMs to solve the "vocabulary problem" in VR interaction, effectively leveraging semantic reasoning to enhance gaze-based interfaces. The reported reduction in arm fatigue and increase in usability are significant strengths, suggesting a strong alignment with natural human behavior. However, the practical deployment of such systems will depend heavily on optimizing the speed of the reasoning loop to ensure seamless immersion.

IRIS: Intent Resolution via Inference-time Saccades for Open-Ended VQA in Large Vision-Language Models

Authors: Parsa Madinei, Srijita Karmakar, Russell Cohen Hoffing et al.
arXiv: 2602.16138v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the challenge of ambiguity in open-ended Visual Question Answering (VQA) for Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs). It investigates whether real-time eye-tracking data can be utilized during inference to resolve user intent without requiring additional model training.

2. Positioning
This research positions itself at the intersection of cognitive science and multimodal AI, addressing the inability of current VLMs to handle vague user intent. It fills a critical gap by proposing gaze as an auxiliary input signal, moving beyond traditional text-and-image-only paradigms. The work suggests that human visual attention serves as a direct proxy for intent, offering a solution where textual queries alone fail.

3. Key Contribution
The main contribution is IRIS, a training-free inference technique that leverages human fixations to clarify ambiguous queries. The authors show that using gaze data from the moment a question is asked more than doubles accuracy on ambiguous tasks, increasing performance from 35.2% to 77.2%. They also provide a new benchmark dataset and a protocol for real-time gaze-based interaction.

4. Methodology
The authors collected synchronized eye-tracking and speech data from a user study involving 500 image-question pairs. They identified that fixations occurring at the onset of the verbal question are the most predictive of user intent. This gaze information is then used to guide the visual attention of the VLM during the inference step, mimicking biological saccades to focus on relevant image regions.

5. Limitations
A primary limitation is the hardware requirement, as the method relies on precise eye-tracking equipment not typically found in standard computing environments. Additionally, the approach depends on the assumption that users consistently look at relevant objects while speaking, which may vary by individual. Real-time processing constraints could also pose challenges for low-latency applications.

6. Critical Evaluation
The paper presents a highly innovative application of gaze data, effectively demonstrating that human visual attention can compensate for model limitations in understanding context. The performance gains are substantial and validate the utility of multimodal biological inputs for AI systems. However, the dependency on specialized hardware limits immediate widespread adoption, and the transferability of this method to standard webcam-based gaze estimation remains an open question.

Human-Centered Multimodal Fusion for Sexism Detection in Memes with Eye-Tracking, Heart Rate, and EEG Signals

Authors: Iván Arcos, Paolo Rosso, Elena Gomis-Vicent
arXiv: 2602.23862v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether integrating human physiological signals, specifically eye-tracking, heart rate, and EEG, with standard visual and textual content can improve the automated detection of sexism in memes. It asks if human cognitive and physiological responses provide an objective signal that content-only models miss.

2. Positioning
This work addresses the limitations of current content-only models, which often struggle with the ambiguity, cultural nuance, and plausible deniability inherent in memes. It positions itself at the intersection of affective computing and content moderation, proposing a human-centered paradigm to bridge the gap between algorithmic processing and complex human perception.

3. Key Contribution
The authors created a novel multimodal dataset by recording eye-tracking, heart rate, and EEG data from subjects viewing memes, providing a unique resource for the community. They demonstrated that sexist memes induce higher cognitive load, evidenced by increased fixation counts and specific EEG spectral power changes. Furthermore, they proposed a fusion model that leverages these physiological signals alongside Vision-Language Model (VLM) features to achieve a measurable improvement in detection accuracy.

4. Methodology
The researchers collected data from 16 subjects viewing 3984 memes, analyzing statistical differences in physiological responses to sexist versus non-sexist content. They developed a multimodal fusion architecture that integrates these physiological signals with enriched textual-visual features extracted from a Vision-Language Model (VLM). This combined approach allows the model to utilize both the raw content of the meme and the implicit human reaction to that content during classification.

5. Limitations
The study relies on a relatively small sample size of 16 subjects, which may limit the generalizability of the physiological patterns identified. Additionally, the reliance on specialized hardware like EEG headsets and eye-trackers makes this approach difficult to deploy in standard, large-scale content moderation systems. The paper also does not deeply explore the potential variance in physiological responses across different demographic groups.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research offers a compelling proof-of-concept that "human-in-the-loop" signals can resolve ambiguities that purely algorithmic models fail to capture. The finding that gaze behavior and neural activity serve as objective markers for perceived sexism is a significant insight for the gaze-tracking community. However, the practical application is currently constrained by the need for physiological hardware, suggesting it is currently more valuable as a tool for understanding human perception and dataset annotation than for real-time moderation.
