Daily Concept: Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction

1. What is it?
Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction refer to the specific ways users distribute their visual attention when engaging with AI systems, particularly Large Language Models. These patterns capture metrics like fixation duration and saccade paths to reveal underlying cognitive processes such as trust, uncertainty, or information processing load during tasks like prompt engineering or output verification.

2. How does it work?
Eye-tracking hardware captures raw coordinate data, which is segmented into fixations and saccades to model visual attention over time. In the context of LLMs, these features are aligned with token generation to identify where users linger or re-read text. Mathematically, the gaze intensity \( G \) over a specific token sequence \( S \) can be modeled as a probability distribution reflecting user interest or confusion. This alignment allows researchers to quantify cognitive load or detect hallucinations by analyzing deviations from expected reading patterns.

3. Why does it matter?
This research bridges the gap between explicit text input and implicit non-verbal signals, allowing AI systems to adapt to user attention in real time. It enables more intuitive interfaces where the model can detect user confusion or verify output accuracy based on reading patterns. Ultimately, it transforms gaze into a passive interaction modality for refining LLM alignment and user experience.

4. Key insight
Gaze acts as a silent feedback loop that reveals user intent and cognitive state faster than explicit text corrections.

Paper Summaries

TextGaze: Prompting Gaze Target Estimation with Textual Scene Cues

Authors: Junhui She, Fei Wang, Kun Li et al.
arXiv: 2607.10130v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the trade-off between complex multi-branch gaze estimation models that require heavy annotation and streamlined models that rely on low-level visual saliency, often missing true gaze intent. It investigates whether Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) can provide semantic guidance to bridge this gap, improving accuracy without increasing annotation costs.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself as a middle ground between supervision-heavy architectures and semantically weak streamlined designs in the gaze estimation literature. It fills a critical gap by demonstrating that textual scene understanding can serve as a scalable substitute for the expensive extra supervision required by previous methods.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is TextGaze, a unified cross-modal architecture that integrates an LVLM to generate textual scene cues for gaze target estimation. The work introduces a novel transformer-based fusion module with hierarchical text supervision, demonstrating that language models can effectively act as semantic guides for visual attention tasks.

4. Methodology
The method extracts visual features using a frozen encoder and utilizes an LVLM to generate gaze-aligned textual cues. These visual and textual modalities are combined in a transformer-based fusion module, which feeds into lightweight decoding heads. The model jointly predicts a gaze heatmap and determines if the target is inside or outside the image frame.

5. Limitations
While the approach improves generalization, the reliance on an LVLM introduces computational overhead compared to lighter, purely visual models. Additionally, the system's performance is inherently bounded by the LVLM's ability to accurately interpret and describe the relevant semantic context of a scene.

6. Critical Evaluation
This paper offers a compelling proof-of-concept for applying LLMs to geometric estimation tasks, successfully moving beyond standard visual saliency methods. The strength of the work lies in its robust cross-dataset generalization without fine-tuning, though the practical deployment may be limited by the inference speed of the Large Vision-Language Model component.

LEXIC: Lightweight Eye-tracking eXtension via Injected Complexity

Authors: Sumin Lee, Kyeonghun Kim, Subeen Lee et al.
arXiv: 2607.08152v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether lightweight, language-model-free conditioning can significantly improve the performance of gaze-only models for predicting reading comprehension. It specifically addresses the performance gap where text-aware models succeed while gaze-only models fail to perform better than chance.

### 2. Positioning
This work positions itself within the EyeBench benchmark context, highlighting a critical disparity where text-aware models utilizing pretrained language models achieve 56-63% AUROC, yet gaze-only baselines operate at chance level. It fills a technical gap by exploring methods to enhance gaze-only architectures without relying on heavy, integrated language models during inference.

### 3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is LEXIC, a method to inject precomputed word-level difficulty signals, specifically GPT-2 surprisal, word frequency, and word length, into a gaze-only model. The authors propose two distinct mechanisms, LEXIC-Concat and LEXIC-Res, demonstrating that these lightweight signals can successfully lift gaze-only models above chance levels on the OneStop reading comprehension task.

### 4. Methodology
The authors modify the AhnCNN baseline by injecting three linguistic features at the fixation level. LEXIC-Concat directly appends these features to the input vector. LEXIC-Res employs a residual approach where a small head predicts a typical gaze response, and the encoder processes the deviation between the actual gaze behavior and the prediction.

### 5. Limitations
The study identifies a transfer limitation in the LEXIC-Res architecture, where the prediction head is calibrated to training readers and struggles with out-of-distribution readers. Additionally, while the method is "language-model-free" during inference, it relies on precomputed features derived from GPT-2, requiring an offline processing step.

### 6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a practical solution for making gaze-only models viable without the computational overhead of running a large language model in real-time. The detailed error analysis of LEXIC-Res provides valuable insights into how reader-specific calibration affects generalization. However, the absolute performance gains remain modest, suggesting that gaze-only features may have an inherent information ceiling for this specific task.

CoMind: Understanding Collaborative Human Activity from Multiple Minds and Views

Authors: Alexey Gavryushin, Dingxi Zhang, Zhao Huang et al.
arXiv: 2607.06691v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the challenge of modeling the cognitive processes, specifically Theory of Mind, that underpin successful collaboration between humans. It investigates how to capture and quantify shared attention and mental states in natural, real world settings to enable AI systems to reason about collaborative behaviors.

2. Positioning
Existing research primarily focuses on modeling physical task execution and coordination while neglecting the underlying cognitive dynamics. This work fills a significant gap by providing a dataset that links observable actions with internal states inferred through gaze tracking. It moves beyond single agent activity recognition to address the complexities of multi agent social interaction.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the CoMind dataset, a multimodal collection featuring synchronized egocentric and exocentric video, gaze tracking, and audio from collaborative cooking scenarios. The authors introduce three novel benchmarks to advance social AI. These benchmarks target Joint Attention Estimation, Socially Conditioned Object Interaction Anticipation, and Collaborative Handover Prediction.

4. Methodology
The researchers collected data from pairs of participants cooking together, capturing synchronized first person and third person video alongside gaze data. They aligned this sensory data with 3D scene scans and manual annotations for social cues and object interactions. This approach allows for the training of models that can correlate gaze patterns with physical actions to predict collaborative intent.

5. Limitations
The dataset is confined to cooking scenarios, which may limit the generalizability of models trained on it to other collaborative domains. The reliance on high fidelity gaze tracking hardware could also pose challenges for deploying these models in standard or low cost environments. Furthermore, the complexity of aligning multimodal data across multiple agents introduces potential noise in the annotations.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work provides a crucial resource for the gaze tracking and embodied AI communities by explicitly linking eye movements to collaborative intent. The strength of the paper lies in its multimodal richness and the definition of tasks that require social reasoning rather than simple pattern recognition. However, the utility of the benchmarks will ultimately depend on the ability of future models to scale effectively to the complexity of unscripted human behavior.

SAGE: Synchronized Action-Gaze Recognition and Anticipation for Human Behavior Understanding

Authors: Chenyi Kuang, Nakul Agarwal
arXiv: 2607.04017v1

Summary

Here is the summary of the research paper.

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the problem of fragmented analysis in human behavior modeling, where gaze and actions are typically treated as separate tasks. It asks whether a unified model can effectively capture the interdependence between human-object interactions and gaze patterns to improve both current recognition and future anticipation.

2. Positioning
Existing literature generally handles gaze estimation and action recognition in isolation, ignoring the cognitive link between where a person looks and what they do. This work fills a critical gap by proposing a holistic approach, arguing that these processes are intricately linked and mutually informative. It positions itself against specialized state-of-the-art models by demonstrating that a unified framework offers superior performance and broader applicability.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is SAGE, a novel end-to-end trainable framework that simultaneously recognizes and anticipates both human-object interactions and gaze behavior. The authors also introduce Exo-Cook, a new benchmark dataset designed to address the scarcity of exocentric video data containing both action and gaze labels. This combination of a unified architecture and a new dataset provides a foundation for more intuitive human-machine interaction.

4. Methodology
The authors utilize a transformer-based architecture that integrates gaze data directly into spatiotemporal attention mechanisms. This allows the model to leverage the bidirectional relationship between visual attention and physical actions, processing visual inputs to predict both current states and future behaviors. The framework is designed to be versatile, functioning effectively in both egocentric views, which focus on detailed hand-eye coordination, and exocentric views, which capture wider contextual scenes.

5. Limitations
While the paper demonstrates strong results, the reliance on gaze data for training implies a dependency on specialized eye-tracking hardware or annotated datasets, which can be difficult to acquire. The performance in highly cluttered or dynamic exocentric environments may face challenges typical of computer vision tasks, such as occlusion or multiple subject interference. Additionally, the generalizability of the new Exo-Cook dataset across diverse, non-kitchen environments remains an area for future validation.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a compelling advancement by validating the intuition that gaze and action are cognitively synchronized, moving beyond siloed analysis. The strength of the approach lies in its ability to leverage gaze as a supervisory signal for action understanding, resulting in a model that often outperforms specialized counterparts. However, the practical adoption of such unified models depends heavily on the availability of high-quality, synchronized gaze data, which remains a bottleneck in many real-world applications.

DeepGaze3.5-VL: Modeling Scanpaths via Autoregressive Token Prediction

Authors: Susmit Agrawal, Matthias Bethge, Matthias Kümmerer
arXiv: 2607.02083v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. How can we overcome the rigid structural biases and limited extendability of traditional scanpath models? The paper aims to solve the problem of flexibly incorporating diverse conditioning factors, such as viewer identity and task instructions, into gaze prediction without requiring custom architectural modifications.

### 2. Positioning. The work positions itself against historical scanpath models that rely on specialized architectures with hand-crafted priors. It fills a gap in the literature by moving away from rigid, custom components and instead framing gaze prediction as a general sequence modeling task. This allows the work to leverage the power of pretrained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) for visual understanding.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is DeepGaze3.5-VL, a model that treats scanpath prediction as an autoregressive token prediction task. This approach achieves state-of-the-art performance with a 46% improvement in Information Gain on the MIT1003 dataset compared to its predecessor. Furthermore, the framework serves as a generative tool for conducting controlled in-silico behavioral experiments that simulate human oculomotor phenomena.

### 4. Methodology. The authors map spatial coordinates and per-fixation attributes, such as duration, into a discrete text vocabulary. By leveraging a pretrained Vision-Language Model, the model predicts the next fixation autoregressively. This formulation allows for flexible conditioning through simple prompting, enabling the model to adapt to specific viewer identities or task objectives without changing the architecture.

### 5. Limitations. While the abstract does not explicitly list limitations, the reliance on discrete tokenization implies a potential loss of spatial precision compared to continuous coordinate regression methods. Additionally, the use of high-capacity Vision-Language Models likely introduces significant computational costs and latency compared to lighter, specialized saliency models.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work represents a significant paradigm shift by successfully unifying gaze prediction with the modern LLM framework of "next token prediction." The ability to recover known oculomotor phenomena through simulation demonstrates that the model learns meaningful representations of human behavior rather than merely fitting data distributions. However, the trade-off between the flexibility of prompting and the computational overhead of large transformer models remains a consideration for real-time applications.
