Daily Concept: Eye tracking in multimodal AI

1. What is it?
Eye tracking in multimodal AI involves integrating gaze data as a distinct input modality alongside text and images within Large Language Models. This technique allows models to leverage human visual attention patterns to better understand user intent or scene context. It transforms gaze coordinates from a purely behavioral metric into a semantic signal for model reasoning.

2. How does it work?
The core mechanism involves encoding raw gaze coordinates and temporal features into vector representations compatible with the model's embedding space. These gaze embeddings are fused with visual or textual tokens, often through cross-attention mechanisms or direct sequence concatenation. Mathematically, the input sequence might be constructed as \(X = [E_{text}; E_{gaze}]\), where \(E_{gaze}\) represents the encoded gaze features. The model then learns to associate specific fixation patterns with relevant semantic content during the pre-training or fine-tuning phase.

3. Why does it matter?
This integration matters because gaze provides an implicit, real-time signal of human cognitive processing and interest that text prompts alone cannot capture. It enables applications like intent prediction, proactive assistance, and accessibility tools for users with motor impairments. Additionally, using gaze as a supervisory signal helps align visual features with linguistic concepts, improving the grounding capabilities of multimodal models.

4. Key insight
Gaze provides a high-bandwidth, implicit signal that allows AI to "see" what the user sees, effectively bridging the gap between human visual perception and machine language generation.

Paper Summaries

Human-Centered Multimodal Fusion for Sexism Detection in Memes with Eye-Tracking, Heart Rate, and EEG Signals

Authors: Iván Arcos, Paolo Rosso, Elena Gomis-Vicent
arXiv: 2602.23862v1

Summary

1. Research Question
Can the accuracy of automated sexism detection in memes be improved by augmenting standard visual and textual analysis with human physiological signals such as eye-tracking and brain activity? The paper investigates whether these biological markers provide an objective measure of human perception that content-only models miss.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself against standard content-only models which often struggle with the ambiguity and cultural nuance of memes. It fills a gap in the literature by moving beyond simple pixel and text analysis to a human-centered approach. The authors argue that current Vision-Language Models (VLMs) fail to capture the full complexity of human perception, specifically the subtle cognitive responses triggered by sexist content.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is a novel dataset resource that maps Eye-Tracking, Heart Rate, and EEG signals to 3984 memes from the EXIST 2025 dataset. The paper also introduces a multimodal fusion model that successfully integrates these physiological signals with VLM features. This approach achieves a notable 3.4% improvement in AUC over strong baselines, with a significant 26.3% boost in F1-score for difficult fine-grained categories.

4. Methodology
The researchers recorded physiological data from 16 subjects viewing memes, identifying statistical links between sexist content and higher cognitive load. They observed that sexist memes triggered increased fixation counts and distinct EEG spectral power patterns. These signals were then fused with enriched textual-visual features from a Vision-Language Model to train a classifier for binary and fine-grained sexism detection.

5. Limitations
The study is limited by a relatively small sample size of only 16 participants, which may restrict the generalizability of the physiological patterns observed. Additionally, the reliance on specialized hardware like EEG headsets and eye-trackers makes this approach difficult to scale for real-time content moderation on consumer devices. The methodology also relies on subjects viewing content in a controlled lab setting, which may not fully reflect casual social media browsing behaviors.

6. Critical Evaluation
This paper presents a fascinating proof-of-concept that effectively quantifies the "gut feeling" humans get when viewing harmful content. The strength of the work lies in its rigorous statistical validation that eye-tracking and EEG metrics serve as objective proxies for perception. However, the practical utility is currently hampered by the hardware requirements, suggesting the most immediate value lies in using this physiological data to train more robust, standard models rather than deploying the sensors themselves.

IRIS: Intent Resolution via Inference-time Saccades for Open-Ended VQA in Large Vision-Language Models

Authors: Parsa Madinei, Srijita Karmakar, Russell Cohen Hoffing et al.
arXiv: 2602.16138v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the challenge of ambiguity in open-ended Visual Question Answering (VQA). It investigates whether real-time eye-tracking data can resolve user intent to improve Large Vision-Language Model (VLM) accuracy without additional training.

2. Positioning
Current Large Vision-Language Models often struggle with ambiguous queries where the user's intent is unclear from the text alone. While previous approaches often require fine-tuning or explicit bounding boxes, this work positions itself as a training-free, intuitive alternative. It bridges the gap between human cognitive intent and machine interpretation by leveraging implicit gaze signals.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is IRIS, a novel, training-free inference method that integrates real-time eye-tracking to disambiguate VQA queries. The authors also provide empirical evidence that fixations synchronized with the start of the verbal question are the most critical for intent resolution. Additionally, they release a new benchmark dataset and evaluation suite to standardize gaze-based VQA research.

4. Methodology
The proposed method captures user gaze fixations during a real-time interactive protocol. It specifically isolates fixations occurring closest to the onset of the verbal question to generate gaze-guided visual prompts. These prompts are then fed into the VLM to ground the visual attention, refining the model's focus without updating model weights.

5. Limitations
The approach requires specialized eye-tracking hardware, which may limit its accessibility compared to standard webcam-based solutions. There is also a dependency on precise temporal synchronization between the gaze data capture and the verbal query onset. Furthermore, the performance relies on the quality of the eye-tracking signal, which can vary across individuals and environmental conditions.

6. Critical Evaluation
The work presents a compelling leap in performance for ambiguous VQA, effectively doubling accuracy without the computational cost of retraining. The focus on the temporal aspect of gaze, specifically the timing relative to speech, offers a nuanced insight into human-computer interaction. However, the practical deployment is constrained by the need for eye-tracking apparatus, potentially limiting its immediate adoption in consumer applications.

ARGaze: Autoregressive Transformers for Online Egocentric Gaze Estimation

Authors: Jia Li, Wenjie Zhao, Shijian Deng et al.
arXiv: 2602.05132v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. How can we accurately predict where a camera wearer is looking in an online egocentric setting using only current and past video frames? The paper aims to solve the problem of estimating visual attention in real-time without access to future frames or explicit eye and head sensors.

### 2. Positioning. This work distinguishes itself from traditional third-person gaze estimation, which typically relies on explicit head and eye signals. It addresses a gap in egocentric vision research where models often struggle to infer gaze from sparse, indirect cues like hand-object interactions. The authors position their approach as a shift from single-frame saliency detection to a temporal sequence modeling problem.

### 3. Key Contribution. The main contribution is ARGaze, a novel framework that reformulates online gaze estimation as an autoregressive sequence prediction task. Drawing inspiration from Large Language Models (LLMs), the model predicts current gaze by conditioning on both current visual features and a history of recent gaze estimates. This approach achieves state-of-the-art results on multiple egocentric benchmarks while strictly adhering to online constraints.

### 4. Methodology. The authors utilize a transformer decoder architecture that functions in an autoregressive manner. At each timestep, the model takes two inputs to predict the current gaze target. These inputs are the visual features extracted from the current frame and a fixed-length "Gaze Context Window" containing past gaze predictions. This design enforces causality, ensuring the model uses only available history to make predictions suitable for streaming inference.

### 5. Limitations. The reliance on a fixed-length context window inherently limits the model's ability to capture long-range temporal dependencies that might extend beyond the window size. Additionally, the autoregressive nature of the model introduces the risk of error propagation. If the model produces an incorrect gaze prediction, that error becomes part of the history context and could degrade the accuracy of future predictions.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling bridge between the fields of gaze estimation and modern sequence modeling techniques used in LLMs. The strength of the paper lies in its intuitive application of temporal continuity, recognizing that human gaze behavior is highly predictable based on recent history. However, the system's dependence on its own previous outputs creates a fragility that could be problematic in noisy real-world environments where early tracking errors might spiral into larger failures.

EyeLayer: Integrating Human Attention Patterns into LLM-Based Code Summarization

Authors: Jiahao Zhang, Yifan Zhang, Kevin Leach et al.
arXiv: 2602.22368v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether human expertise in code understanding, proxied by eye-tracking data, can enhance the performance of Large Language Models (LLMs) on the task of code summarization. It specifically asks if integrating human attention patterns can improve the semantic focus and output quality of these models.

2. Positioning
While LLMs have achieved significant success in code summarization, current models rely solely on data-driven attention mechanisms that may miss subtle semantic cues humans naturally prioritize. This work positions itself at the intersection of software engineering and cognitive computing, filling the gap by integrating biological attention signals as a complementary prior to standard model training.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is EyeLayer, a lightweight module that seamlessly injects human gaze patterns into various LLM architectures without disrupting their pre-trained representations. The authors demonstrate that this biological attention signal is highly transferable, achieving consistent performance improvements across encoder-only models like CodeBERT and decoder-only models like LLaMA.

4. Methodology
EyeLayer models human attention using a Multimodal Gaussian Mixture, utilizing learned parameters \((\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)\) to represent the location and intensity of developer focus. This mechanism redistributes token embeddings to prioritize areas where humans gaze most intently, effectively augmenting the model's internal attention weights with external cognitive data.

5. Limitations
Although the abstract highlights strong results, the approach relies on the availability of high-quality eye-tracking datasets, which are resource-intensive to collect compared to standard code corpora. Potential limitations include the variability of gaze patterns among developers with different expertise levels and the technical challenge of accurately aligning gaze fixations with specific code tokens.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a compelling fusion of cognitive science and deep learning, providing a novel way to "humanize" model attention without expensive architectural overhauls. The reported BLEU-4 gains of up to 13.17% are impressive, though the practical dependency on eye-tracking hardware could pose a barrier to widespread adoption compared to purely software-based optimization techniques.

On the scaling relationship between cloze probabilities and language model next-token prediction

Authors: Cassandra L. Jacobs, Morgan Grobol
arXiv: 2602.17848v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates why larger language models correlate better with human reading behavior. It specifically asks how model scale influences the relationship between model predictions and human cloze probabilities.

2. Positioning
This research builds on established findings that larger language models predict human reading times and eye movements more accurately than smaller ones. It fills a gap by explaining the specific mechanisms driving this improvement, specifically contrasting semantic alignment against low-level lexical sensitivity.

3. Key Contribution
The authors demonstrate that larger models provide higher-quality estimates of next-token probabilities in cloze tasks by prioritizing semantic appropriateness over simple lexical co-occurrence statistics. A key insight is that increased memorization capacity in larger models aids semantic guessing but reduces sensitivity to the low-level information relevant for word recognition.

4. Methodology
The authors compare next-token prediction probabilities from language models of varying sizes against human-generated cloze probability data. They analyze the alignment between model outputs and human responses by evaluating semantic fit and sensitivity to lexical co-occurrence statistics.

5. Limitations
The abstract notes that even the most advanced models still under-allocate probability mass to actual human responses, indicating a persistent gap in modeling human uncertainty. Additionally, the focus on cloze tasks as a proxy for reading behavior may not fully capture the complexity of real-time eye-tracking metrics.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a compelling mechanistic explanation for why scaling laws hold in psycholinguistic modeling, moving beyond simple correlation analysis. It successfully bridges NLP and cognitive science by identifying the specific trade-offs involved in model scaling, though the reliance on cloze data limits direct application to low-level gaze control.
