Daily Concept: Fixation detection algorithms

### 1. What is it?
Fixation detection algorithms are computational methods used to classify raw eye-tracking data into meaningful gaze states. They primarily distinguish between fixations, where the gaze holds relatively still to process visual information, and saccades, which are rapid movements between these points. This process filters out measurement noise and physiological jitter to isolate the specific moments when visual attention occurs.

### 2. How does it work?
These algorithms typically operate by analyzing the velocity or spatial dispersion of gaze coordinates over time. The Velocity-Threshold Identification (I-VT) method is a standard approach that calculates the angular velocity \( v_i \) between consecutive gaze points \( p_i \) and \( p_{i-1} \). This is often computed as. \[ v_i = \frac{\| p_i - p_{i-1} \|}{\Delta t} \]
where \( \Delta t \) is the time difference between samples. If the velocity \( v_i \) falls below a predefined threshold \( \lambda \), the algorithm classifies that time segment as a fixation. Other methods use dispersion thresholds to group points that stay within a small spatial area.

### 3. Why does it matter?
In the context of LLMs, fixation detection is the critical preprocessing step that aligns continuous gaze data with discrete text tokens. It enables the mapping of specific reading behaviors, such as regression or re-reading, directly to words or sub-word units. This alignment allows researchers to train models on human attention patterns or use gaze features to improve model efficiency and interpretability.

### 4. Key insight. Fixation detection effectively serves as the tokenization layer for eye-tracking data, translating continuous biological signals into the discrete units required for language model integration.

Paper Summaries

Decoding Pedestrian Crossing Intention from Egocentric Vision via Vision Language Models

Authors: Danya Li, Xiang Su, Yan Feng et al.
arXiv: 2606.09142v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. Can Vision Language Models (VLMs) effectively decode pedestrian crossing intentions from short egocentric video clips? The paper investigates whether these models can outperform traditional methods and how specific contextual cues, particularly eye gaze, influence prediction accuracy.

### 2. Positioning. The authors position their work at the intersection of egocentric vision and traffic safety, noting that the potential of first-person views for safety prediction is underexplored. They address the gap between general-purpose VLMs and specialized traffic models by framing the prediction task as a Visual Question Answering (VQA) problem rather than a standard classification task.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is the demonstration that parameter-efficient fine-tuning enables VLMs to surpass specialized transformer baselines in intent prediction. The study establishes a new state of the art by showing that incorporating eye gaze and ego motion improves accuracy by 14.5% over the baseline, highlighting the value of multimodal physiological cues.

### 4. Methodology. The authors formulate intent prediction as a closed-ended VQA task and benchmark several state-of-the-art VLM families in a zero-shot setting. They then apply parameter-efficient fine-tuning to adapt the models to the target domain. Finally, they integrate contextual cues such as ego motion, vehicle motion, and eye gaze into the model inputs to enhance predictive performance.

### 5. Limitations. The authors acknowledge that zero-shot VLMs exhibit limited higher-level traffic reasoning, achieving only moderate gains over random guessing. This indicates that while VLMs are powerful, they lack innate spatial and safety awareness without specific domain adaptation. The reliance on fine-tuning also suggests that pre-trained general models cannot be deployed "out of the box" for this high-stakes safety task.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling proof of concept for integrating gaze tracking with Large Language Model architectures to solve complex prediction problems. The significant performance boost provided by eye gaze reinforces the importance of attention mechanisms in modeling human intent. However, the reliance on fine-tuning suggests that current VLMs still struggle to generalize their "world knowledge" to specialized, safety-critical egocentric scenarios without targeted training data.

DeepGaze3.5-VL: Modeling Scanpaths via Autoregressive Token Prediction

Authors: Susmit Agrawal, Matthias Bethge, Matthias Kümmerer
arXiv: 2607.02083v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the limitations of traditional scanpath models, which rely on rigid, specialized architectures that are difficult to extend or condition on different contexts. It investigates whether framing scanpath prediction as a discrete sequence modeling task using Vision-Language Models (VLMs) can improve performance and flexibility.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself as a departure from historical scanpath models that depend on hand-crafted priors and structural biases. It fills a critical gap by offering a unified framework that absorbs diverse factors of variation, removing the need for custom architectural additions to handle different tasks or viewer identities.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is DeepGaze3.5-VL, a model that treats gaze coordinates and durations as text tokens to leverage pretrained VLM representations. This approach achieves state-of-the-art performance with a 46% improvement in Information Gain over the previous DeepGaze III model. It also introduces a generative framework capable of simulating complex behavioral interventions that are difficult to perform in real-world experiments.

4. Methodology
The authors map continuous spatial coordinates and temporal attributes into a discrete text vocabulary. They utilize an autoregressive Vision-Language Model to predict these tokens, allowing for flexible conditioning through simple text prompts. This formulation enables the scalable computation of log-likelihoods, which are directly equivalent to the Information Gain metric used in eye-tracking research.

5. Limitations
While the abstract highlights performance gains, potential limitations include the computational cost associated with high-capacity Vision-Language Models compared to traditional lightweight architectures. The discretization of continuous coordinate data into tokens may also introduce quantization errors, potentially limiting the spatial precision of the predictions compared to continuous regression methods.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research represents a significant paradigm shift in gaze modeling by successfully unifying visual attention prediction with the versatility of Large Language Model architectures. The strength of the approach lies in its flexibility and the ability to perform "in-silico" experiments, though it trades the efficiency of specialized models for the generality of foundation models.

Same Scrutiny, More Time: Eye Tracking Insights into Reviewing LLM-Labelled Code

Authors: Ranim Khojah, Francisco Gomes de Oliveira Neto, Mazen Mohamad et al.
arXiv: 2606.26505v1

Summary

1. Research Question
How does explicitly labeling code as LLM-generated influence a software engineer's review behavior and attention? The paper investigates whether the "AI-generated" label causes developers to scrutinize code differently compared to code they believe is human-written.

2. Positioning
While previous research has established that LLMs can produce erroneous code, there is little empirical evidence on how developers actually behave when inspecting such code in practice. This work fills a gap between recommended verification protocols and actual human behavior by using objective physiological measures rather than relying solely on self-reported surveys.

3. Key Contribution
The study reveals that labeling code as LLM-generated increases the time developers spend fixating on the code, even though the overall thoroughness or coverage of the review remains the same. This finding highlights a discrepancy between intent and action, as developers adapt their strategies by focusing on logical correctness or using the original prompt to guide their inspection.

4. Methodology
The researchers conducted a Wizard-of-Oz experiment where participants reviewed code that was explicitly labeled as LLM-generated. They utilized eye-tracking technology to capture quantitative behavioral data, such as fixation duration and scan patterns, and supplemented this with qualitative data from exit interviews. Bayesian data analysis was then used to interpret the gaze metrics and identify statistically significant differences in attention.

5. Limitations
The study acknowledges that the lab environment may not fully replicate the time pressures and contextual factors of professional software development. The specific participant pool and the artificial nature of the review task might limit the generalizability of the findings to broader engineering teams. Additionally, the Wizard-of-Oz setup might introduce biases if participants suspected the true nature of the experiment.

6. Critical Evaluation
This paper provides a strong methodological contribution by successfully applying eye-tracking to the emerging problem of human-LLM interaction in software engineering. The distinction found between time spent and actual coverage is a nuanced insight that challenges the assumption that more time automatically equals better review quality. However, the study could be strengthened by a deeper analysis of specific gaze patterns to determine exactly which code elements drew the extra attention.

Retrieval-Grounded Multilingual LLM Assistance for Island Smallholder Farmers

Authors: Nikolaos D. Tantaroudas, Ilias Karachalios, Andrew J. McCracken
arXiv: 2606.25647v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the challenge of providing reliable agricultural advice to smallholder farmers in remote areas where local knowledge and regional dialects are absent from global LLM training data. It investigates how to build a trustworthy, grounded AI assistant for low-resource contexts without the overhead of self-hosting models.

2. Positioning
The authors position their work against general-purpose chatbots that hallucinate facts and against complex self-hosted open-source solutions. They fill a gap in the literature by demonstrating a lightweight, retrieval-augmented architecture suitable for resource-constrained rural deployments.

3. Key Contribution
The key contribution is the "Falco eleonorae" system, a multilingual, multimodal assistant embedded in an e-market platform. Its novelty lies in using a Model Context Protocol tool to ground LLM responses in geospatially bounded, curated local data rather than relying on fine-tuning or vector databases.

4. Methodology
The system utilizes a thin Backend-for-Frontend proxy that delegates text generation to managed GPT-5 models and speech-to-text to an EU streaming service. It employs tool-augmented retrieval where a geospatial interface exposes local agronomic data via Well-Known Text envelopes to anchor the model's context.

5. Limitations
The primary limitation is the reliance on external managed services, which creates dependencies on network connectivity and third-party availability. Additionally, the system's effectiveness is contingent on the quality of the curated local database and the proprietary model's ability to interpret specific regional dialects accurately.

6. Critical Evaluation
This paper provides a valuable applied engineering perspective on making LLMs accessible and trustworthy for specialized, low-resource domains. While it lacks novel model architectures or gaze-based interaction mechanisms, its strength lies in the practical integration of geospatial data and retrieval tools to solve a real-world digital divide problem.

When LLM Rationales Become User-Facing: Effects on Trust Perception, Decision-Making, and Gaze Behaviors

Authors: Xin Sun, Ting Pan, Yajing Wang et al.
arXiv: 2606.25489v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether user-facing LLM rationales facilitate auditable trust calibration or merely persuade users through fluent reasoning. It specifically asks how rationale correctness, presentation timing, and certainty framing influence user trust, decision-making, and cognitive processing.

2. Positioning
This work addresses the gap between internal model capabilities and user interface design in Explainable AI (XAI). It challenges the prevailing assumption that exposing step-by-step reasoning always benefits the user. The authors position their research within the context of "auditable trust calibration," arguing that rationales should enable users to inspect evidence rather than simply accepting fluent text.

3. Key Contribution
The key contribution is empirical evidence that incorrect rationales reduce trust in the LLM system more than providing no rationale at all. The study uniquely links eye-tracking metrics, such as pupil diameter and fixation duration, to user trust states. It also demonstrates that gaze features can predict user trust and decision confidence, providing a physiological basis for evaluating interface designs.

4. Methodology
The authors conducted two studies involving a factual verification task. Study 1 was an online experiment manipulating rationale format, correctness, and certainty framing to measure subjective trust. Study 2 utilized eye-tracking with 54 participants to analyze gaze behaviors and pupil dilation across different rationale conditions.

5. Limitations
The sample sizes for both studies (N=68 and N=54) are relatively small, potentially limiting the statistical power and generalizability of the findings. The research focused solely on a factual verification task, so the results may not translate to more open-ended or creative LLM applications. The study also used simulated rationales rather than outputs from a specific live model, which might not capture the full variability of LLM errors.

6. Critical Evaluation
This paper provides a vital corrective to the trend of always displaying reasoning chains, offering concrete physiological evidence that bad explanations incur high cognitive costs. The integration of gaze analysis is a major strength, providing objective data to support subjective trust reports. However, the reliance on simulated rationales limits the ecological validity regarding how users interact with the specific error modes of current state-of-the-art models.
