Daily Concept: Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction

1. What is it?
Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction refer to the specific ways users visually attend to AI-generated content, revealing underlying cognitive states like trust, confusion, or verification effort. This concept involves tracking eye movements such as fixations and saccades while users read, edit, or evaluate outputs from Large Language Models. It effectively treats the eye as a rich input signal for understanding how humans process and collaborate with intelligent systems.

2. How does it work?
The mechanism relies on capturing the spatial and temporal coordinates of a user's gaze $g_t = (x, y, t)$ and mapping them onto the text tokens $T_i$ displayed on a screen. Researchers extract features like total fixation duration and regression count to quantify cognitive load and reading comprehension depth. In the context of LLMs, the probability of a user fixating on a specific token $P(fix|T_i)$ often correlates with the token's perceived information density or error likelihood. This allows systems to distinguish between smooth reading patterns and disrupted scanning indicative of confusion.

3. Why does it matter?
This matters because gaze provides an implicit, real-time feedback signal that can be used to align LLM outputs with human intent. By analyzing where users linger or exhibit regressive eye movements, models can learn to identify hallucinations or confusing phrasing without requiring explicit user ratings. It bridges the gap between explicit text prompts and the implicit cognitive reactions of the user.

4. Key insight
Gaze data serves as a proxy for human attention and cognitive processing, offering a window into user perception that precedes explicit verbal or written feedback.

Paper Summaries

LEXIC: Lightweight Eye-tracking eXtension via Injected Complexity

Authors: Sumin Lee, Kyeonghun Kim, Subeen Lee et al.
arXiv: 2607.08152v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether a gaze-only model can significantly improve its performance on reading comprehension tasks by using lightweight, language-model-free conditioning signals. It specifically asks if injecting precomputed word difficulty metrics can close the performance gap between gaze-only models and heavier text-aware models.

2. Positioning
Current research highlights a major disparity where text-aware models using pretrained language models achieve 56 to 63 percent AUROC, while gaze-only models perform at chance level. This work positions itself as a bridge between these extremes, exploring how to enhance gaze-only baselines without relying on full language model integration.

3. Key Contribution
The main contribution is LEXIC, a lightweight extension that injects three precomputed word-level signals, GPT-2 surprisal, word frequency, and word length, into gaze-only models. The authors introduce two injection mechanisms, direct concatenation and a residual deviation predictor, demonstrating statistically significant AUROC gains on the OneStop reading comprehension benchmark.

4. Methodology
Building on the EyeBench AhnCNN baseline, the authors propose LEXIC-Concat, which appends difficulty signals directly to the input, and LEXIC-Res, which conditions the encoder on the deviation between actual gaze data and a predicted typical reader response. These models were evaluated on the OneStop task using a 5-seed ensemble across ten folds to measure performance on unseen text and unseen readers.

5. Limitations
The residual mechanism, LEXIC-Res, struggles to generalize to unseen readers because its prediction head is calibrated to the specific readers encountered during training. Additionally, while the model is lightweight, it still relies on precomputed GPT-2 surprisal values, introducing a dependency on external language model data.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a practical and efficient solution to the poor performance of gaze-only models by leveraging linguistic features without the computational cost of end-to-end language models. However, the reliance on GPT-2 surprisal suggests that the model is not entirely language-model-free, potentially limiting its applicability in scenarios where such precomputed data is unavailable.

CoMind: Understanding Collaborative Human Activity from Multiple Minds and Views

Authors: Alexey Gavryushin, Dingxi Zhang, Zhao Huang et al.
arXiv: 2607.06691v1

Summary

Here is a structured summary of the research paper.

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the difficulty of studying cognitive processes, specifically Theory of Mind, during natural human collaboration. It asks how we can effectively model the mental states and shared attention that underpin successful teamwork in real world settings.

2. Positioning
Existing research has largely concentrated on modeling physical coordination and task execution, often overlooking the internal cognitive mechanisms that drive collaboration. This work fills a critical gap by providing a dataset specifically designed to analyze the "mind" behind the actions, moving beyond simple activity recognition to reasoning about intent and shared understanding.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the CoMind dataset, a comprehensive multimodal resource capturing real world cooking collaboration through synchronized egocentric and exocentric views. It uniquely integrates gaze tracking data with high quality audio and 3D scene scans to enable the study of shared attention. The authors also establish three specific benchmarks including Joint Attention Estimation and Collaborative Handover Prediction to standardize evaluation in this domain.

4. Methodology
The researchers collected data using head mounted cameras and eye trackers on participants performing cooking tasks alongside external static cameras. This setup allowed for the capture of multi perspective video and gaze data, which was then temporally aligned with audio and 3D object scans. The data was annotated for shared attention, social cues, and agent object interactions to support the proposed benchmarks.

5. Limitations
While the dataset is rich, it is currently limited to cooking scenarios, which may restrict the generalizability of models trained on this data to other collaborative domains. The reliance on head mounted gaze tracking hardware also presents a potential limitation for applications requiring passive, non intrusive observation in strictly natural settings. Additionally, the complexity of the multimodal data requires significant computational resources for processing.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work represents a significant step forward for embodied AI and human robot interaction by prioritizing gaze as a primary signal for modeling Theory of Mind. The combination of ego and exo views addresses the limitations of single perspective datasets, offering a more complete picture of collaborative dynamics. However, the specificity of the cooking domain means that models developed here will need rigorous testing to prove their robustness in more diverse or unstructured real world environments.

SAGE: Synchronized Action-Gaze Recognition and Anticipation for Human Behavior Understanding

Authors: Chenyi Kuang, Nakul Agarwal
arXiv: 2607.04017v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper addresses the problem of disjointed modeling in computer vision, where human actions and gaze patterns are typically processed independently. It asks whether a unified model can effectively leverage the interdependence between gaze and actions to improve the simultaneous recognition and anticipation of human behavior.

### 2. Positioning. Existing literature typically treats gaze estimation and action recognition as isolated tasks, ignoring the cognitive link between where a person looks and what they do. This work fills that gap by proposing a solution that captures the bidirectional relationship between visual attention and physical interaction. It also extends the field beyond the common egocentric (first-person) view to include exocentric (third-person) perspectives.

### 3. Key Contribution. The main contribution is SAGE, a transformer-based framework that unifies Human Object Interaction (HOI) and gaze recognition with anticipation capabilities in a single end-to-end trainable model. The authors also introduce Exo-Cook, a new benchmark dataset designed to facilitate research on gaze and action in exocentric videos. The paper demonstrates that this joint modeling approach consistently outperforms specialized state-of-the-art models across multiple datasets.

### 4. Methodology. The approach employs a transformer architecture that integrates gaze data directly into spatiotemporal attention mechanisms. This design allows the model to process video inputs and generate synchronized predictions for both current and future actions alongside gaze coordinates. By treating gaze and action as interconnected signals within the attention layers, the system learns shared representations that benefit both tasks.

### 5. Limitations. While the authors introduce Exo-Cook to address data scarcity, this dataset is specific to cooking scenarios, which may limit the immediate generalizability of the findings to other complex activities. Additionally, unified multi-task models often require more computational resources and intricate tuning than simpler, specialized models. The reliance on high-quality gaze annotations for training also remains a standard constraint for data-hungry transformer architectures.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This research offers a compelling argument for multi-task learning by validating the intuitive connection between gaze and action. The strength of the work lies in its versatility, showing strong performance in both egocentric and exocentric views, which broadens its potential application in robotics and AR. However, the dependency on a new, domain-specific dataset (Exo-Cook) suggests that the model's robustness in diverse, uncontrolled real-world environments needs further verification.

DeepGaze3.5-VL: Modeling Scanpaths via Autoregressive Token Prediction

Authors: Susmit Agrawal, Matthias Bethge, Matthias Kümmerer
arXiv: 2607.02083v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the limitations of existing scanpath models, which rely on rigid, specialized architectures that are difficult to extend to new tasks or viewer conditions. It investigates whether framing scanpath prediction as a discrete sequence modeling task using Vision-Language Models (VLMs) can improve predictive performance and flexibility.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself against traditional scanpath models that require hand-crafted priors and custom architectural components for specific tasks, such as integrating viewer identity or task instructions. It fills a gap in the literature by proposing a unified framework that leverages the generalization capabilities of pretrained VLMs, allowing for flexible conditioning without modifying the model architecture.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is DeepGaze3.5-VL, a model that treats gaze coordinates and fixation durations as a text vocabulary to predict scanpaths autoregressively. This approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets, including a 46% improvement in Information Gain on the MIT1003 benchmark over the previous DeepGaze III model. Additionally, the framework enables exact log-likelihood computation and allows for controlled in-silico simulations of oculomotor behaviors.

4. Methodology
The authors map continuous spatial coordinates and temporal attributes, such as fixation duration, into a discrete vocabulary of text tokens. These tokens are processed by a VLM which uses the image context and preceding tokens to predict the next fixation autoregressively. This formulation allows researchers to condition the model on global factors, such as viewer identity or search tasks, using simple text prompts.

5. Limitations
While the abstract highlights significant achievements, the conversion of continuous gaze coordinates into a discrete vocabulary inherently introduces a loss of spatial precision due to quantization. Furthermore, relying on large pretrained Vision-Language Models likely incurs higher computational costs compared to lighter, specialized gaze prediction architectures.

6. Critical Evaluation
This paper represents a significant methodological shift in eye-tracking research, successfully demonstrating that Large Language Model techniques can outperform domain-specific architectures. The ability to prompt the model with viewer IDs or task descriptions offers a scalable path toward personalized and task-aware gaze modeling. However, the trade-off between the flexibility of discrete token prediction and the spatial precision required for fine-grained gaze analysis remains a key consideration for future applications.

Same Scrutiny, More Time: Eye Tracking Insights into Reviewing LLM-Labelled Code

Authors: Ranim Khojah, Francisco Gomes de Oliveira Neto, Mazen Mohamad et al.
arXiv: 2606.26505v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates how software engineers behave when code is explicitly labeled as LLM-generated during a code review task. It asks whether the "AI-generated" label influences the reviewer's attention, time spent, and review strategy compared to standard code.

2. Positioning
As LLMs become common in software development, existing literature highlights the need for careful inspection due to model hallucinations. This work fills a gap by moving beyond self-reported surveys to objective behavioral data, exploring the discrepancy between the recommended scrutiny and actual reviewer behavior.

3. Key Contribution
The study reveals that while reviewers spend more time fixating on LLM-labeled code, the overall thoroughness or coverage of the review does not increase. A novel finding is that practitioners adapt their strategy by using the original prompt as a guide for verification, suggesting the prompt should be treated as a key software artifact.

4. Methodology
The authors conducted a Wizard-of-Oz experiment where participants reviewed code labeled as LLM-generated. They collected objective behavioral data using eye-tracking technology to measure fixation duration and attention allocation, complemented by exit interviews and Bayesian data analysis.

5. Limitations
The controlled experimental setting may not fully capture the complex dynamics and time pressures of real-world professional software development. Additionally, the specific code snippets used and the participant demographics might limit the generalizability of the findings across different programming contexts.

6. Critical Evaluation
This paper offers a significant methodological contribution by applying eye-tracking to quantify the cognitive load and attention patterns associated with AI-generated code. The discovery of a gap between increased fixation time and unchanged review coverage highlights a critical challenge for tool designers, suggesting that simply labeling code as AI-generated is insufficient to ensure rigorous verification.
