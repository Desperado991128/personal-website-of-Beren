Daily Concept: Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction

1. What is it?
Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction refer to the specific eye movement behaviors, such as fixations and saccades, exhibited by users while reading, evaluating, or prompting AI systems. In the context of LLMs, this involves analyzing how humans visually process machine-generated text compared to human-written text. It serves as a proxy for cognitive load, attention, and trust during collaborative tasks with AI.

2. How does it work?
The process begins with eye-tracking hardware recording the user's point of gaze on a screen, capturing raw coordinates and pupil dilation at high frequencies. Algorithms process this raw data into fixations, which are then mapped to specific tokens in the LLM input or output. Researchers often model this relationship by treating gaze features as continuous variables that predict human attention or perceived quality. For example, a feature vector \(g_i\) for a token \(t_i\) might include fixation duration \(d\) and pupil diameter \(p\), such that \(g_i = [d_i, p_i]\).

3. Why does it matter?
This research area is crucial for understanding the cognitive load required to interpret AI outputs and for identifying where LLMs fail to align with human expectations. By quantifying how users read machine-generated text, researchers can fine-tune models to produce more fluent or trustworthy responses. It also enables the development of implicit feedback loops where user attention signals help correct model hallucinations without explicit user intervention.

4. Key insight
Gaze patterns serve as an implicit, real-time signal for evaluating AI performance, revealing user confusion or trust before the user explicitly provides feedback.

Paper Summaries

Decoding Pedestrian Crossing Intention from Egocentric Vision via Vision Language Models

Authors: Danya Li, Xiang Su, Yan Feng et al.
arXiv: 2606.09142v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether Vision Language Models (VLMs) can effectively decode pedestrian crossing intentions from short egocentric video clips. It asks if formulating this prediction task as a Visual Question Answering (VQA) problem can outperform traditional specialized architectures.

2. Positioning
The authors position this work at the intersection of egocentric vision and traffic safety prediction, a domain they identify as underexplored. They address the limitations of existing specialized transformer-based models by proposing a more flexible approach using general-purpose VLMs.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is establishing a new state-of-the-art result for egocentric pedestrian intent decoding using the Qwen3-VL-2B model. The study demonstrates that incorporating eye gaze and ego motion as contextual cues improves accuracy by 14.5% over a specialized transformer baseline. This highlights the potential of combining multimodal LLMs with behavioral signals like gaze for safety-critical applications.

4. Methodology
The authors reformulated the intention prediction task into a closed-ended Visual Question Answering (VQA) framework. They first benchmarked state-of-the-art VLMs in a zero-shot setting, then applied parameter-efficient fine-tuning to adapt the models to the target domain. The methodology uniquely integrates auxiliary data streams, specifically eye gaze, ego motion, and vehicle motion, to guide the model's reasoning process.

5. Limitations
The study reveals that off-the-shelf VLMs possess limited higher-level reasoning for traffic scenarios, performing only moderately better than random guessing without fine-tuning. Additionally, the reliance on short video clips may restrict the model's ability to capture long-term temporal dependencies relevant to pedestrian behavior.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work provides a compelling proof-of-concept for leveraging VLMs in dynamic, real-world prediction tasks. The explicit inclusion of eye gaze as a guiding signal is a significant strength, aligning AI attention mechanisms with human visual behavior. However, the heavy reliance on fine-tuning suggests that current general-purpose models still lack the innate spatial reasoning required for complex traffic interactions.

Same Scrutiny, More Time: Eye Tracking Insights into Reviewing LLM-Labelled Code

Authors: Ranim Khojah, Francisco Gomes de Oliveira Neto, Mazen Mohamad et al.
arXiv: 2606.26505v1

Summary

1. Research Question
This research investigates how software engineers behave when code is explicitly labeled as LLM-generated during a code review task. The core question asks whether the "LLM-generated" label influences the reviewer's attention, time spent, and review strategy compared to unlabeled code.

2. Positioning
While existing literature emphasizes the need for careful inspection of LLM-generated code due to potential hallucinations, empirical evidence regarding actual developer behavior remains scarce. This work fills a critical gap by moving beyond theoretical recommendations to observe real-time review behaviors using objective physiological measures.

3. Key Contribution
The study provides empirical evidence that while the overall thoroughness of code review remains consistent, developers spend significantly more time fixating on code labeled as LLM-generated. It also identifies that practitioners adapt their strategies by using the original prompt as a guide for verification, highlighting a disconnect between review intentions and actual behavior.

4. Methodology
The researchers employed a Wizard-of-Oz experiment where participants reviewed code that was explicitly labeled as LLM-generated. They collected behavioral data using eye-tracking technology to measure fixation duration and distribution, combining this quantitative data with qualitative insights from exit interviews and Bayesian analysis.

5. Limitations
The study acknowledges a gap between the reviewers' intentions to be thorough and their actual reviewing behavior, suggesting that self-reported diligence may not match objective metrics. Additionally, the specific "LLM-generated" label used in the experiment might elicit different behaviors than proprietary or more trusted AI tools in production environments.

6. Critical Evaluation
The use of eye-tracking provides a robust, objective measure of attention that strengthens the findings beyond typical self-reported surveys. However, the distinction between increased fixation time and unchanged thoroughness is nuanced, suggesting that while the AI label triggers caution, it may not necessarily lead to more effective error detection.

Retrieval-Grounded Multilingual LLM Assistance for Island Smallholder Farmers

Authors: Nikolaos D. Tantaroudas, Ilias Karachalios, Andrew J. McCracken
arXiv: 2606.25647v1

Summary

Here is a structured summary of the research paper.

### 1. Research Question. The paper addresses the challenge of providing reliable agricultural advice to smallholder farmers in remote areas where local dialects and specific agronomic knowledge are absent from standard Large Language Model (LLM) training data. It asks how a conversational AI assistant can be designed to provide accurate, grounded support for resource-constrained, bilingual rural environments.

### 2. Positioning. This work positions itself as an alternative to general-purpose chatbots that often hallucinate due to a lack of local context. It fills a gap in the literature regarding AI deployment for low-resource, dialect-specific agricultural communities. The authors argue that for small-scale rural deployments, a managed service with robust grounding is more practical and trustworthy than self-hosted open-source models.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is the design and deployment of "Falco eleonorae", a retrieval-grounded, multilingual AI assistant tailored for island farmers. It introduces a system architecture that leverages the Model Context Protocol (MCP) to ground LLM responses in authoritative local data rather than relying solely on pre-trained weights. The system also integrates multilingual voice and image capabilities within a low-bandwidth Progressive Web App.

### 4. Methodology. The authors propose a thin Backend-for-Frontend (BFF) proxy architecture that delegates language generation to managed OpenAI GPT-5 models. Grounding is achieved through tool-augmented retrieval, where an MCP tool queries a curated database of local crops, practices, and dialect terms wrapped in geospatial envelopes. The system handles voice via a streaming speech-to-text service and images via a vision model that converts field photos into text descriptions for the agent.

### 5. Limitations. The system relies on managed upstream services, introducing dependencies on external providers and requiring stable internet connectivity for the backend. The reliance on a manually curated local data interface implies a maintenance overhead for keeping the knowledge base current. Additionally, the specific focus on a single island's dialect and agricultural context may limit the generalizability of the solution to other regions without significant reconfiguration.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work presents a pragmatic and robust solution for a high-impact real-world problem, effectively bridging the gap between powerful general LLMs and hyper-local knowledge. The architecture is clever in its use of geospatially-aware retrieval tools to constrain model outputs, which ensures trustworthiness. However, the reliance on proprietary models raises questions about long-term cost sustainability and data sovereignty for sensitive agricultural data.

When LLM Rationales Become User-Facing: Effects on Trust Perception, Decision-Making, and Gaze Behaviors

Authors: Xin Sun, Ting Pan, Yajing Wang et al.
arXiv: 2606.25489v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether user-facing LLM rationales help users accurately verify information or merely persuade them through fluent reasoning. It specifically asks how rationale correctness, presentation timing, and certainty framing influence user trust, decision-making, and cognitive effort.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself at the intersection of Explainable AI (XAI) and Human-Computer Interaction (HCI), addressing the trend of making internal LLM reasoning visible to users. It fills a gap in the literature by moving beyond algorithmic performance to examine the human side of auditable trust calibration, specifically how users process and verify these rationales behaviorally.

3. Key Contribution
The key contribution is empirical evidence that incorrect rationales trigger higher cognitive effort and reduce system trust, challenging the assumption that showing reasoning steps is always beneficial. The paper also demonstrates that eye-tracking metrics, such as pupil diameter and attention to evidence, serve as reliable predictors of user trust and decision states.

4. Methodology
The authors conducted two linked studies involving a factual verification task. Study 1 was an online experiment manipulating rationale formats, while Study 2 was a controlled eye-tracking experiment analyzing gaze patterns and pupil dilation to measure cognitive load and attention distribution.

5. Limitations
The study is limited by its specific focus on factual verification tasks, which may not generalize to more open-ended or creative LLM interactions. Additionally, the sample sizes of 68 and 54 participants, while sufficient for controlled studies, limit the ability to detect smaller or more nuanced interaction effects.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a vital behavioral perspective on LLM explainability by utilizing gaze data to quantify cognitive effort, moving beyond subjective self-reports. A potential weakness is that the "rationales" used were likely curated or simulated for the experiment, which might not fully capture the unpredictability of real-time LLM generation errors.

Energy-Based Transformers as Predictors of Reading Difficulty

Authors: Jakub Dotlacil, Ece Takmaz
arXiv: 2606.23382v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper investigates whether "energy," a measure derived from energy-based Transformers, can serve as a robust predictor of human reading difficulty. It specifically asks if this single metric can unify or replace existing complementary measures like surprisal and attention entropy.

### 2. Positioning. The authors position their work at the intersection of computational psycholinguistics and deep learning theory. They bridge the gap between modern language models and classical cognitive theories by linking Transformers to Hopfield networks and associative memory models. This fills a void in the literature, as energy-based measures have not previously been applied to predict reading behavior.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is the demonstration that the energy measure is a robust predictor of reading times across multiple corpora, including eye-tracking datasets. The paper shows that energy provides a significant fit beyond surprisal and subsumes the effects of both surprisal and attention entropy. This suggests that energy can function as a single unified predictor where multiple measures were previously required.

### 4. Methodology. The authors utilize energy-based Transformers to derive an energy score for input sequences, theoretically grounding this in associative memory models. They validate this measure by correlating it with reading times from the Natural Stories corpus, the UCL eye-tracking corpus, and the UCL self-paced reading corpus. They also perform a controlled analysis on relative clause processing to test for specific syntactic effects.

### 5. Limitations. As the "first exploration" of this technique in psycholinguistics, the generalizability of the findings across different model architectures and languages remains to be fully tested. The observation that energy works best at a "single layer" suggests that identifying the correct layer for analysis might be a sensitive or model-dependent parameter. Furthermore, the paper does not deeply address the computational cost or complexity of training these specific energy-based models compared to standard Transformers.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling theoretical advancement by connecting Transformer mechanics to established memory theories, providing a more elegant explanation of processing load. The finding that a single metric can capture effects previously attributed to multiple distinct factors is a strong step toward simplifying models of human sentence processing. However, the practical utility for the broader gaze-tracking community will depend on how easily these energy measures can be extracted from standard, large-scale pre-trained models compared to traditional surprisal calculations.
