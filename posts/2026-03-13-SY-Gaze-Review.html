Daily Concept: Gaze patterns in human-AI interaction

1. What is it?
Gaze patterns in human AI interaction refer to the distinct visual attention behaviors users exhibit when engaging with systems powered by large language models. This concept involves analyzing where, how long, and in what sequence users look at AI generated text or interface elements. It treats eye movements as a rich source of implicit feedback regarding user intent, comprehension, and cognitive load.

2. How does it work?
Eye tracking hardware captures raw ocular data streams which are then processed into fixation durations and saccade amplitudes while a user reads or verifies model outputs. Researchers map these spatial coordinates onto the tokenized input sequence \( T = \{t_1, t_2, \dots, t_n\} \) to create a corresponding gaze feature sequence \( G = \{g_1, g_2, \dots, g_n\} \). Machine learning models utilize this alignment to predict human reading patterns or to identify tokens that cause processing difficulty. This mechanism allows the AI to mathematically incorporate human attention signals into its optimization loop.

3. Why does it matter?
This concept matters because gaze data provides a continuous, implicit signal of user attention and confusion without interrupting the interaction flow. It enables the development of LLMs that optimize for human readability and cognitive ease rather than just statistical likelihood. Applications include gaze assisted text generation, real time error correction, and adaptive interfaces that respond to user fatigue or misunderstanding.

4. Key insight
Gaze patterns bridge the gap between implicit human cognition and explicit machine computation. They allow AI systems to optimize for how humans actually read and process information, not just what they explicitly type or click.

Paper Summaries

SIAgent: Spatial Interaction Agent via LLM-powered Eye-Hand Motion Intent Understanding in VR

Authors: Zhimin Wang, Chenyu Gu, Feng Lu
arXiv: 2603.00522v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the high learning costs and low error tolerance associated with current VR interaction paradigms that require users to memorize specific gesture-command mappings. It investigates how to shift from a rigid "Operation-to-Intent" model to a flexible "Intent-to-Operation" model where natural eye-hand motions directly convey user goals.

2. Positioning
This work challenges the dominant "Operation-to-Intent" paradigm in VR, which forces users to learn predefined gestures. It positions itself as a more intelligent alternative that leverages Large Language Models to bridge the gap between raw spatial data and semantic user intent. This fills a critical gap in making VR interfaces more intuitive by removing the need for gesture memorization.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the SIAgent framework, which uses LLMs to translate spatial eye-hand data into natural language intents for task execution. This novel approach eliminates the need for predefined gestures, achieving a recognition accuracy of 97.2% compared to 93.1% for standard gaze-and-pinch methods. It also significantly reduces arm fatigue and improves system usability.

4. Methodology
The proposed system features two main components. The first is an intent recognition module that translates spatial interaction data into natural language to infer user goals using an LLM. The second is an agent-based execution module that generates and performs the corresponding tasks in the virtual environment based on the recognized intent.

5. Limitations
The abstract does not explicitly list limitations, but the reliance on LLMs for processing spatial data implies potential latency challenges for real-time VR applications. Additionally, the system's accuracy may vary depending on the ambiguity of natural user motions or the specific capabilities of the LLM used for intent inference.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research offers a compelling application of LLMs to gaze-tracking and VR, successfully demonstrating that semantic understanding can outperform deterministic gesture mapping. The reported improvements in accuracy and fatigue reduction are significant strengths that validate the "Intent-to-Operation" approach. However, the practical success of this method relies heavily on the inference speed and robustness of the LLM component to handle diverse, real-world user behaviors.

IRIS: Intent Resolution via Inference-time Saccades for Open-Ended VQA in Large Vision-Language Models

Authors: Parsa Madinei, Srijita Karmakar, Russell Cohen Hoffing et al.
arXiv: 2602.16138v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the challenge of ambiguity in open-ended Visual Question Answering (VQA) for Large Vision-Language Models. It investigates whether real-time human gaze data can be leveraged during inference to resolve user intent and improve answer accuracy without requiring model retraining.

2. Positioning
This research positions itself at the intersection of cognitive science and multimodal AI, addressing the limitation of current VLMs that struggle with underspecified or ambiguous queries. It fills a critical gap by proposing an inference-time solution that utilizes implicit human attention signals, rather than relying on expensive model fine-tuning or complex prompt engineering. The work establishes a new protocol for human-AI interaction by treating eye-tracking not just as an analysis tool, but as an active input signal.

3. Key Contribution
The authors introduce IRIS, a novel training-free technique that integrates real-time eye-tracking to disambiguate visual questions. They provide empirical evidence that gaze fixations synchronized with the start of a verbal question are highly predictive, boosting accuracy on ambiguous questions from \(35.2\%\) to \(77.2\%\). Additionally, they release a benchmark dataset and evaluation suite to standardize future research in gaze-augmented VQA.

4. Methodology
IRIS operates by monitoring user eye movements during the question formulation phase. It identifies the specific visual fixations that occur closest to the moment the user begins verbally asking their question. The model then uses this spatial data to focus its attention on the relevant image region, effectively filtering out irrelevant visual noise to resolve the ambiguity.

5. Limitations
The approach inherently requires specialized eye-tracking hardware, which limits its immediate applicability on standard consumer devices like laptops or smartphones. There is also an assumption that users naturally fixate on the object of interest while speaking, a behavior that might vary across individuals or complex question types. Furthermore, the real-time synchronization of gaze data and audio input introduces system latency constraints that must be managed carefully.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work presents a compelling argument for integrating physiological signals into LLM reasoning workflows, offering a highly effective training-free method to solve a persistent problem in VQA. The reported doubling of accuracy on ambiguous tasks is a significant result that highlights the untapped potential of gaze data. However, the practical barrier of hardware requirements suggests that future work must focus on making such gaze-sensing capabilities more accessible through webcams or standard sensors.

N-gram-like Language Models Predict Reading Time Best

Authors: James A. Michaelov, Roger P. Levy
arXiv: 2603.09872v1

Summary

1. Research Question
Why do advanced transformer models often struggle to predict human reading times compared to simpler models? The paper asks if reading time metrics are actually more sensitive to simple n-gram statistics than the complex patterns learned by state-of-the-art neural networks.

2. Positioning
This work addresses a puzzling trend in psycholinguistics where improvements in next-word prediction accuracy do not translate to better predictions of eye-tracking measures. It fills a gap by challenging the direct application of large language models as cognitive models of reading.

3. Key Contribution
The authors show that neural models whose probability estimates resemble simple n-grams are the most effective at predicting reading times. This implies that human reading behavior is largely driven by shallow statistical regularities rather than the deep contextual reasoning of modern transformers.

4. Methodology
The researchers analyze the correlation between the probability outputs of various neural language models and traditional n-gram models. They then compare these alignments against eye-tracking data from naturalistic text to determine which modeling approach best predicts reading duration.

5. Limitations
The reliance on reading time as a primary metric may obscure other cognitive processes involved in text comprehension that are not captured by n-gram statistics. The study also potentially underestimates the role of long-distance dependencies that transformers handle well but n-grams do not.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research provides a crucial reality check for the use of LLMs in cognitive science, highlighting that bigger or newer models are not always better for explaining human behavior. However, the binary comparison between n-gram and transformer features might miss intermediate cognitive mechanisms that explain why humans process language efficiently.

GazeMoE: Perception of Gaze Target with Mixture-of-Experts

Authors: Zhuangzhuang Dai, Zhongxi Lu, Vincent G. Zakka et al.
arXiv: 2603.06256v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper addresses the challenge of estimating human gaze targets from visible images in a way that generalizes well across different scenarios. Specifically, it investigates how to effectively integrate multi-modal cues, such as eye features and head pose, into a pre-trained vision foundation model without compromising its existing knowledge.

### 2. Positioning. The authors position their work at the intersection of gaze estimation and the emerging field of large vision foundation models. They identify a gap in existing literature where, despite the power of pre-trained models, there lacks an efficient mechanism to decode and adapt multi-modal gaze cues from these heavy architectures. The work fills this void by adapting the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) paradigm, commonly used in Large Language Models (LLMs), to create an adaptive decoding process for gaze estimation.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is GazeMoE, a novel end-to-end framework that applies Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) modules to a frozen vision foundation model. This architecture allows the system to selectively activate specific "experts" to process relevant gaze cues, such as head pose or context, improving efficiency and accuracy. Additionally, the authors introduce a class-balancing auxiliary loss and specific data augmentation strategies to solve the common problem of class imbalance between in-frame and out-of-frame gaze targets.

### 4. Methodology. The proposed method keeps the backbone of a pre-trained vision foundation model frozen to preserve its general feature extraction capabilities. It attaches Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) adapter modules which learn to route different types of information to specialized expert networks. To further refine the training, the authors employ region-specific cropping and photometric transformations as data augmentations, alongside a loss function designed to balance the classification of gaze targets.

### 5. Limitations. While the abstract demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, it does not detail the computational overhead or inference latency introduced by the MoE layers compared to simpler adapter methods. Additionally, the reliance on a frozen foundation model may inherently limit the model's ability to adapt to domain-specific nuances that differ significantly from the pre-training data distribution.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling synthesis of modern LLM scaling techniques, specifically Mixture-of-Experts, applied to a classic computer vision problem. The strength of the paper lies in its efficient adaptation strategy, which achieves high performance without the prohibitive cost of fine-tuning an entire large model. However, the practical deployment value would be clearer with a detailed analysis of the trade-off between the performance gains and the increased parameter count associated with the expert modules.

Thinking with Gaze: Sequential Eye-Tracking as Visual Reasoning Supervision for Medical VLMs

Authors: Yiwei Li, Zihao Wu, Yanjun Lv et al.
arXiv: 2603.06697v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. How can we improve the visual reasoning capabilities of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) in radiology tasks where standard textual reasoning is suboptimal? The core question asks whether sequential eye-tracking data from radiologists can serve as effective supervision to guide model reasoning and evidence acquisition.

### 2. Positioning. Current VLMs typically process images as visual tokens but rely heavily on textual intermediate reasoning, creating a disconnect for visually grounded tasks like radiology. This work positions itself against standard text-only reasoning approaches by leveraging the temporal dynamics of expert gaze trajectories. It fills a critical gap by attempting to align the model's internal processing with the sequential visual search patterns used by human experts.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is the introduction of dedicated "gaze tokens" trained to predict gaze-selected image patch indices in temporal order. This approach forces the model to mimic human visual search strategies rather than relying solely on textual inference. The method achieves state-of-the-art in-domain performance on MIMIC-EYE and demonstrates improved robustness on external zero-shot benchmarks.

### 4. Methodology. The authors integrate a small set of special tokens into the VLM architecture that are explicitly trained to output indices of image patches corresponding to radiologist fixations. By ordering these predictions temporally, the model learns to follow the sequential evidence acquisition process observed in eye-tracking data. This supervision mechanism anchors the model's reasoning chain directly to visual regions of interest over time.

### 5. Limitations. The approach relies on the availability of high-quality, time-ordered eye-tracking data, which is significantly scarcer and more expensive to collect than standard image-text pairs. Additionally, the method assumes that mimicking human gaze trajectories is the optimal reasoning path for the model, potentially limiting performance if the model could theoretically discover more efficient or distinct visual strategies.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling bridge between cognitive science and deep learning by treating gaze not just as attention, but as a temporal reasoning process. The strength lies in validating that explicit visual supervision improves robustness and out-of-domain generalization. However, the dependency on granular eye-tracking datasets poses a significant scalability challenge for broader application outside of specialized medical domains.
