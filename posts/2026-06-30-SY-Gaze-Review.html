Here are selected research papers at the intersection of gaze/eye-tracking and LLMs for June 30, 2026.

Daily Concept: Eye tracking in multimodal AI

1. What is it?
Eye tracking in multimodal AI involves integrating gaze data as an explicit input signal alongside text and images. This approach allows models to leverage human attention mechanisms, such as fixations and saccades, to guide processing and improve understanding of visual or textual content. It essentially treats the human eye movement as a supplementary information channel that directs the model's focus.

2. How does it work?
The system captures raw gaze coordinates and temporal features like fixation duration, then projects them into the model's embedding space using learned linear layers or transformers. These gaze embeddings are fused with visual or textual tokens, often through cross-attention mechanisms, to align the model's internal focus with human attention. Mathematically, this often involves weighting visual features \( V \) by a gaze probability map \( G \) to generate an attended representation \( V' = V \odot G \). This process enables the Large Language Model to prioritize specific image regions or text tokens that humans found relevant during training or inference.

3. Why does it matter?
Incorporating gaze signals allows AI models to mimic human efficiency by focusing computational resources on salient regions rather than processing entire scenes uniformly. It improves model interpretability by revealing which features drive decisions and enhances performance in tasks requiring fine-grained visual search or reading comprehension. This intersection bridges the gap between human cognitive processes and machine reasoning, enabling more natural human-AI collaboration.

4. Key insight
Gaze data acts as a powerful supervisory signal that guides the model's attention mechanism to relevant features, effectively distilling human cognitive intent into machine learning pipelines.

Paper Summaries

Decoding Pedestrian Crossing Intention from Egocentric Vision via Vision Language Models

Authors: Danya Li, Xiang Su, Yan Feng et al.
arXiv: 2606.09142v1

Summary

1. Research Question
Can Vision Language Models (VLMs) effectively decode pedestrian crossing intentions from short egocentric video clips? The paper investigates whether formulating this prediction task as a Visual Question Answering (VQA) problem allows VLMs to outperform traditional specialized architectures.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself at the intersection of egocentric vision and traffic safety prediction, a domain where the potential of first person views remains largely untapped. It fills a critical gap by moving beyond specialized transformer based models, exploring instead how general purpose VLMs handle complex decision making tasks when augmented with behavioral cues like eye gaze.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is establishing a new state of the art for egocentric pedestrian intent decoding by demonstrating that fine tuned VLMs significantly outperform specialized baselines. A key novelty is the successful integration of multimodal contextual cues, specifically showing that eye gaze and ego motion are vital for improving the predictive accuracy of language vision models.

4. Methodology
The authors approach intent prediction as a closed ended VQA task, prompting the model to answer whether a pedestrian will cross based on video input. They benchmark state of the art VLMs in a zero shot setting and subsequently apply parameter efficient fine tuning. The methodology uniquely incorporates auxiliary data streams, specifically ego motion, vehicle motion, and eye gaze, to provide the model with explicit attentional and kinetic context.

5. Limitations
The study reveals that standard zero shot VLMs possess limited higher level traffic reasoning, achieving only moderate gains over random guessing without specific training. Additionally, the reliance on precise eye gaze and ego motion data requires specialized hardware and data processing, which may limit the applicability of this approach in scenarios where such sensor data is unavailable or noisy.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research provides a compelling proof of concept for integrating physiological signals like eye gaze with large vision language models. It effectively demonstrates that implicit attention mechanisms in VLMs benefit significantly from explicit gaze supervision. However, the dependence on fine tuning and specific sensor inputs suggests that generalizing this solution to diverse, unconstrained real world environments remains a challenge.

Same Scrutiny, More Time: Eye Tracking Insights into Reviewing LLM-Labelled Code

Authors: Ranim Khojah, Francisco Gomes de Oliveira Neto, Mazen Mohamad et al.
arXiv: 2606.26505v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates how software engineers behave when code is explicitly labeled as LLM-generated during a code review task. It asks whether this labeling influences the reviewer's attention, time spent, and review strategy compared to code not labeled as AI-generated.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself at the intersection of software engineering and human-computer interaction, addressing the gap between recommended verification practices for AI code and actual developer behavior. It moves beyond self-reported surveys by using physiological measures to understand the cognitive impact of AI labeling.

3. Key Contribution
The study reveals that while the "LLM-generated" label does not change the thoroughness of the review, it causes developers to spend significantly more time fixating on the code. It also identifies that developers shift their review strategy by focusing on logical correctness or using the original prompt as a contextual artifact.

4. Methodology
The researchers conducted a Wizard-of-Oz experiment where participants reviewed code snippets that were labeled as either human-written or LLM-generated. They collected behavioral data using eye-tracking technology to measure gaze patterns and fixation duration, alongside qualitative data from exit interviews.

5. Limitations
The study acknowledges a disconnect between the participants' intent to be thorough and their actual measured behavior, suggesting that self-reporting alone is insufficient for studying this phenomenon. Additionally, the controlled experimental environment may not fully capture the complex pressures and time constraints of real-world industrial software development.

6. Critical Evaluation
The integration of eye-tracking provides objective, high-fidelity data that successfully highlights a discrepancy between review time and actual scrutiny. However, the interpretation that increased fixation time equates to attention rather than confusion warrants further investigation to optimize AI code review tools.

Retrieval-Grounded Multilingual LLM Assistance for Island Smallholder Farmers

Authors: Nikolaos D. Tantaroudas, Ilias Karachalios, Andrew J. McCracken
arXiv: 2606.25647v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the lack of reliable agricultural advice available to smallholder farmers in remote, depopulating areas. It asks how a conversational AI can be grounded in local, authoritative data and regional dialects that are typically absent from the training corpora of general-purpose Large Language Models.

2. Positioning
This work positions itself as an alternative to both general-purpose chatbots, which lack local context, and resource-intensive self-hosted models. It fills a gap in the literature by demonstrating a lightweight, retrieval-grounded architecture specifically designed for resource-constrained, bilingual rural environments.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is Falco eleonorae, a multilingual AI assistant embedded within an island-specific e-market platform. The system introduces a novel grounding mechanism using a Model Context Protocol tool to query a curated, geospatially bounded dataset, ensuring the model operates strictly within the area of interest.

4. Methodology
The authors propose a thin Backend-for-Frontend proxy that delegates core tasks to managed services like OpenAI GPT-5 models and an EU streaming speech-to-text service. The system grounds responses via tool-augmented retrieval from a read-only data interface where entries are wrapped in geospatial Well-Known Text envelopes. It also employs a vision-capable model to transcribe uploaded field photographs into text for the agent to process.

5. Limitations
The system relies heavily on managed upstream services, which introduces dependencies on external providers and potential data sovereignty concerns. The effectiveness of the assistant is also strictly limited by the scope and quality of the manually curated local database and the accuracy of the speech-to-text service regarding regional dialects.

6. Critical Evaluation
This paper offers a pragmatic and scalable solution for domain-specific LLM deployment, prioritizing accessibility and low bandwidth over complex model hosting. While it lacks the gaze-tracking interaction analysis often found in advanced HCI research, its multimodal design effectively addresses the digital divide for non-technical users in isolated communities.

When LLM Rationales Become User-Facing: Effects on Trust Perception, Decision-Making, and Gaze Behaviors

Authors: Xin Sun, Ting Pan, Yajing Wang et al.
arXiv: 2606.25489v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper investigates whether user-facing LLM rationales help users accurately calibrate trust or merely persuade them through fluent reasoning. It asks how rationale correctness and presentation affect user trust, decision-making, and cognitive effort.

### 2. Positioning. LLMs increasingly display reasoning steps to users, yet it remains unclear if this transparency aids verification or simply creates an illusion of competence. This work positions itself at the intersection of explainable AI and trust calibration. It fills a gap by examining the behavioral and physiological costs of processing incorrect rationales.

### 3. Key Contribution. The main contribution is the finding that incorrect rationales increase cognitive load and reduce system trust more than showing no rationale at all. The paper also demonstrates that gaze data, specifically pupil diameter and attention to evidence, serves as a predictive signal for user trust states. This challenges the prevailing design trend of always showing reasoning steps.

### 4. Methodology. The authors conducted two linked studies. Study 1 used an online experiment to manipulate rationale presentation formats and correctness levels. Study 2 employed eye-tracking to measure gaze behaviors and pupil dilation while users evaluated factual claims with varying rationale conditions.

### 5. Limitations. The study focuses specifically on factual verification, which may not extend to creative or complex reasoning tasks. The sample sizes of 68 and 54 participants were relatively small, potentially limiting statistical power. Furthermore, the use of pre-defined rationales might not fully replicate the experience of dynamic, AI-generated explanations.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. The integration of eye-tracking provides a robust objective measure of cognitive load that strengthens the subjective trust findings. The paper offers a crucial counter-narrative to the "more explanation is better" paradigm in AI design. A weakness is the constrained task environment, which may limit applicability to broader LLM use cases.

Energy-Based Transformers as Predictors of Reading Difficulty

Authors: Jakub Dotlacil, Ece Takmaz
arXiv: 2606.23382v1

Summary

1. Research Question
Can an energy-based measure derived from transformer models serve as a unified predictor of human reading difficulty? The authors investigate whether this single metric can outperform or subsume the traditional combination of surprisal and attention entropy.

2. Positioning
Current computational psycholinguistics relies on surprisal and attention entropy as complementary predictors of processing load. This work bridges the gap between modern language models and classical cognitive science by connecting transformers to Hopfield networks and associative memory models. It fills a theoretical gap by offering a principled, unified metric where previously multiple distinct measures were required.

3. Key Contribution
The paper introduces the "energy" measure from energy-based transformers as a novel predictor for reading times. It demonstrates that this measure robustly predicts reading difficulty across three major corpora, including the UCL eye-tracking dataset. Crucially, the authors show that energy captures effects previously attributed to both surprisal and attention entropy, potentially eliminating the need for multiple complementary metrics.

4. Methodology
The authors utilize energy-based transformers, which map transformer operations to energy functions in associative memory models. They extract energy values from specific layers to serve as regressors for reading times in the Natural Stories, UCL eye-tracking, and UCL self-paced reading corpora. They also test the measure on a controlled experiment regarding relative clause processing to verify it captures specific syntactic asymmetries.

5. Limitations
The study relies on specific energy-based transformer architectures, which may not perfectly reflect the internal dynamics of the larger standard transformers used in mainstream NLP. While the energy measure correlates with reading times, the precise cognitive mechanism it represents requires further theoretical interpretation. Additionally, the research focuses on English datasets, potentially limiting generalizability to languages with different morphological or syntactic structures.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a compelling theoretical advance by linking deep learning architectures to classical memory models. The strength lies in its ability to simplify the predictive framework by replacing two metrics with one that performs better. However, the practical adoption of this metric depends on the wider availability and scalability of energy-based transformer implementations compared to standard pre-trained models.
