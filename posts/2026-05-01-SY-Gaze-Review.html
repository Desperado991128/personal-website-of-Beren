Daily Concept: Fixation detection algorithms

### 1. What is it?
Fixation detection algorithms are computational methods used to identify periods when the eye gaze remains relatively stationary on a specific location. They transform raw, noisy eye-tracking data streams into discrete fixation events while filtering out rapid eye movements called saccades. This process acts as a critical preprocessing step that converts continuous coordinate data into meaningful units of visual attention.

### 2. How does it work?
The most common approach is the Velocity-Threshold Identification (I-VT) classifier, which classifies gaze points based on the angular velocity between successive samples. The algorithm calculates the instantaneous velocity \( v \) between points \( p_i \) and \( p_{i+1} \) using the Euclidean distance divided by the time difference. If the calculated velocity is below a predefined threshold \( \lambda \), the samples are grouped as a fixation.
\[
v = \frac{\|p_{i+1} - p_i\|}{\Delta t}
\]
Alternatively, dispersion-based methods (I-DT) group points that stay within a small spatial radius for a minimum duration duration.

### 3. Why does it matter?
These algorithms are essential for isolating moments of actual cognitive processing from motor noise and rapid movements. For LLM research, detected fixations serve as the ground truth tokens of human attention, allowing researchers to map biological reading patterns onto artificial attention mechanisms. This mapping enables the training of gaze-augmented models that mimic human reading efficiency or provides a benchmark for evaluating how models allocate attention compared to humans.

### 4. Key insight. Fixation detection acts as the tokenization layer for visual attention, converting continuous biological signals into discrete data structures that language models can process and analyze.

Paper Summaries

SGAP-Gaze: Scene Grid Attention Based Point-of-Gaze Estimation Network for Driver Gaze

Authors: Pavan Kumar Sharma, Pranamesh Chakraborty
arXiv: 2604.19888v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the limitations of current driver gaze estimation models that rely exclusively on driver facial information. It investigates whether integrating external traffic scene context with internal driver facial cues can improve the accuracy of Point-of-Gaze (PoG) estimation.

2. Positioning
Existing literature predominantly focuses on predicting gaze direction or PoG using only facial features, ignoring the environmental context that influences where a driver looks. This work fills that gap by introducing a scene-aware approach, arguing that surrounding traffic cues are critical for modeling driver attention. It positions itself as a multi-modal solution that bridges the divide between internal driver state and external scene context.

3. Key Contribution
The authors introduce a new benchmark dataset called Urban Driving-Face Scene Gaze (UD-FSG) containing synchronized face and scene images. They also propose SGAP-Gaze, a novel network that fuses facial features with scene context using a Transformer-based attention mechanism. This approach achieves a 23.5% reduction in mean pixel error compared to state-of-the-art models, demonstrating particular strength in estimating gaze toward critical outer regions of the scene.

4. Methodology
The proposed SGAP-Gaze network extracts and fuses features from the driver's face, eyes, and iris to form a "gaze intent vector." This vector interacts with a spatial grid representation of the driving scene through a Transformer-based attention mechanism. The model computes attention scores over the scene grid to fuse facial intent with environmental context, predicting the final Point-of-Gaze coordinates.

5. Limitations
The abstract does not explicitly list limitations, but the reliance on a proprietary dataset (UD-FSG) may limit immediate reproducibility using older benchmarks. Additionally, while the model performs better on outer regions, gaze targets in these areas are described as rare, suggesting that data imbalance remains an inherent challenge in the training distribution.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work presents a significant advancement by shifting the paradigm from purely physiological gaze tracking to a holistic, context-aware model. The reported 23.5% error reduction validates the hypothesis that scene context is a powerful predictor for driver attention. The architecture effectively leverages modern Transformer attention mechanisms to align driver intent with the visual environment, offering a robust direction for future driver monitoring systems.

Object Referring-Guided Scanpath Prediction with Perception-Enhanced Vision-Language Models

Authors: Rong Quan, Yantao Lai, Dong Liang et al.
arXiv: 2604.20361v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. The paper addresses the problem of Object Referring-guided Scanpath Prediction (ORSP). The core research question asks how to accurately predict the sequence of human eye fixations when a person searches for a specific object described by a text prompt within a visual scene.

### 2. Positioning. This work positions itself at the intersection of computer vision, natural language processing, and eye-tracking research. It fills a gap in existing literature by moving beyond traditional scanpath prediction models to specifically handle multimodal inputs where linguistic guidance directs visual search. The authors identify that existing methods struggle with effectively fusing visual and linguistic features and lack fine-grained positional awareness.

### 3. Key Contribution. The main contribution is the proposal of ScanVLA, a novel framework that leverages a Vision-Language Model (VLM) to extract and fuse aligned multimodal features. The authors introduce a History Enhanced Scanpath Decoder (HESD) to utilize past fixation coordinates for better sequential prediction. Additionally, they integrate a frozen Segmentation LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) module to enhance the model's perception of object boundaries without significant computational overhead.

### 4. Methodology. The ScanVLA model processes the input image and referring expression through a VLM to generate fused feature representations. It employs the proposed History Enhanced Scanpath Decoder (HESD) which takes the positional information of previous fixations as input to predict the next fixation point. To improve spatial accuracy, the method utilizes a frozen Segmentation LoRA as an auxiliary component, helping the model localize the target object more precisely during the scanpath generation.

### 5. Limitations. The provided abstract does not explicitly list specific limitations of the proposed method. However, potential limitations implied by the methodology include a reliance on the quality of the pre-trained VLM and the Segmentation LoRA. While the auxiliary module is efficient, the base VLM architecture may still require substantial computational resources compared to lighter, non-VLM baseline models.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work represents a significant advancement in multimodal gaze prediction by successfully adapting Large Vision-Language Models for the specific task of visual search. The strength of the approach lies in its dual enhancement of semantic understanding via the VLM and positional awareness via the HESD and Segmentation LoRA. By demonstrating that scanpath prediction can be improved through auxiliary segmentation tasks without heavy computational penalties, the paper offers a valuable contribution to the intersection of LLMs and eye-tracking research.

Understanding Password Preferences, Memorability, and Security through a Human-Centered Lens

Authors: Duru Paker, Suleyman Ozdel, Enkelejda Kasneci
arXiv: 2604.19410v2

Summary

### 1. Research Question. How do user behavior and visual attention during password creation impact the security and memorability of passwords generated by humans versus AI models? This study investigates the effectiveness of AI-based generators and the role of gaze behavior in the security-usability trade-off.

### 2. Positioning. This research positions itself at the intersection of usable security and generative AI. It addresses a gap in the literature regarding the effectiveness of LLM-based password generators and the lack of behavioral analysis during the password creation process. By applying eye-tracking, it moves beyond simple performance metrics to understand the cognitive processes behind password selection.

### 3. Key Contribution. The study confirms that while LLMs generate stronger passwords, a significant trade-off exists with human memorability. A key novel contribution is the discovery that visual attention to contextual website cues correlates with higher password entropy. This suggests that security is influenced by where users look, opening the door for attention-driven authentication interfaces.

### 4. Methodology. The authors conducted an eye-tracking experiment involving participants creating passwords for four simulated website contexts. They compared three LLM-based generators (DeepSeek, ChatGPT, PassGPT) with a rule-based random generator. Eye movements were recorded to measure visual attention during the creation, selection, and memorization phases.

### 5. Limitations. The abstract does not explicitly state limitations, but the reliance on simulated contexts may affect the generalizability of the results to real-world scenarios. The finding that users prefer self-generated passwords despite AI strength suggests a behavioral barrier that technical solutions alone may not overcome. Furthermore, the study focuses on immediate memorability which may differ from long-term retention.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This paper provides a valuable human-centered perspective on the application of LLMs in security. The integration of gaze data to link visual attention with password strength is a sophisticated methodological choice that yields actionable design insights. However, the persistence of user preference for self-generated passwords indicates that improving generator algorithms is only one part of a complex usability challenge.

Probing for Reading Times

Authors: Eleftheria Tsipidi, Samuel Kiegeland, Francesco Ignazio Re et al.
arXiv: 2604.18712v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. This paper investigates whether the internal representations of Large Language Models (LLMs) capture cognitive signals related to human reading behavior. Specifically, it asks if these representations can predict human reading times more effectively than traditional metrics like surprisal.

### 2. Positioning. While previous research has established that probing LLM representations reveals linguistic knowledge, it remains unclear if these models encode cognitive processing data found in human behavior. This work fills that gap by connecting the field of representational probing with psycholinguistic modeling. It positions LLM embeddings as potential predictors of human gaze behavior alongside established theories like surprisal.

### 3. Key Contribution. The primary contribution is the discovery that representations from early model layers predict early reading measures, such as first fixation and gaze duration, better than standard surprisal scores. This suggests a functional alignment where low-level model layers correspond to the early temporal stages of human reading. Additionally, the study shows that combining surprisal with early-layer representations creates a superior predictive model for reading times.

### 4. Methodology. The authors employ probing classifiers using regularized linear regression to predict eye-tracking metrics from LLM hidden states across five languages. They compare the predictive power of representations from every model layer against scalar predictors, specifically surprisal, information value, and logit-lens surprisal. This setup allows them to map specific model layers to different temporal stages of the human reading process.

### 5. Limitations. A key limitation is that scalar surprisal remains the superior predictor for late-pass measures like total reading time, suggesting LLM representations do not fully capture deeper cognitive integration processes. The study also notes that the best-performing predictor varies significantly depending on the specific language and eye-tracking measure. This variability indicates that a universal model of cognitive processing based solely on LLM embeddings is not yet feasible.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a compelling psycholinguistic perspective on LLM interpretability by linking model depth to the temporal evolution of human reading. The finding that early layers predict early gaze measures provides a nuanced understanding of how LLMs might mirror human cognitive architectures. However, the reliance on regularized linear regression may limit the ability to capture complex non-linear relationships between representations and reading times.

Employing General-Purpose and Biomedical Large Language Models with Advanced Prompt Engineering for Pharmacoepidemiologic Study Design

Authors: Xinyao Zhang, Nicole Sonne Heckmann, Manuela Del Castillo Suero et al.
arXiv: 2604.17988v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether Large Language Models (LLMs) can reliably automate the design of pharmacoepidemiologic studies. It specifically asks how general-purpose models compare to specialized biomedical models in terms of relevance, reasoning, and ontology coding accuracy.

2. Positioning
This research addresses the growing but under-characterized interest in using AI for pharmaceutical study design. It fills a critical gap by providing a comparative analysis between widely used general-purpose models and niche biomedical models, an area where performance data was previously lacking.

3. Key Contribution
The study demonstrates that general-purpose models like GPT-4o currently outperform specialized biomedical LLMs for study design tasks. It also establishes that advanced prompt engineering techniques, specifically Least-to-Most prompting, significantly improve reasoning stability and output quality.

4. Methodology
The authors evaluated four distinct LLMs using a dataset of 46 protocols sourced from the HMA-EMA Catalogue and Sentinel System. The evaluation framework employed Least-to-Most and Active Prompting strategies to assess performance across relevance, logic of justification, and ontology-code agreement.

5. Limitations
A primary limitation noted in the results is that all models struggled significantly with ontology-code mapping, indicating a lack of proficiency in precise technical coding tasks. Additionally, the biomedical models tested frequently provided insufficient justification for their outputs, limiting their utility in high-stakes scientific design.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a pragmatic and slightly counter-intuitive insight that larger, general-purpose models currently beat smaller, fine-tuned alternatives in this domain. However, the persistent failure in ontology mapping highlights that LLMs are not yet ready for fully autonomous deployment in rigorous scientific regulatory processes.
