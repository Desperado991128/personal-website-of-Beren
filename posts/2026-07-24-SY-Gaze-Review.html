Here are selected research papers at the intersection of gaze/eye-tracking and LLMs for July 24, 2026.

Daily Concept: Gaze-based intention recognition

1. What is it?
Gaze-based intention recognition is the computational task of inferring a user's latent goals or planned actions based on their eye movement patterns. In the context of Large Language Models (LLMs), it serves as an implicit input modality that allows the model to understand user intent without requiring explicit text commands.

2. How does it work?
The algorithm processes raw eye-tracking data to extract spatiotemporal features such as fixation durations and saccade velocities. These features are encoded into a latent representation that is aligned with the LLM's embedding space or mapped to a probability distribution over potential intentions. The inference is typically formalized as estimating the optimal intention $I^*$ given a sequence of gaze observations $\mathbf{G}$.
\[ I^* = \arg\max_{I} P(I | \mathbf{G}) \]

3. Why does it matter?
This approach enables proactive AI assistance by allowing systems to react to user attention and interest in real time. It significantly reduces interaction friction and expands accessibility for users who cannot easily type or speak commands. It also provides LLMs with crucial context about user focus to improve the relevance of generated responses.

4. Key insight
Eye movements act as a silent signal that bridges the gap between human cognitive states and machine understanding, turning attention into a controllable interface for AI.

Paper Summaries

TextGaze: Prompting Gaze Target Estimation with Textual Scene Cues

Authors: Junhui She, Fei Wang, Kun Li et al.
arXiv: 2607.10130v1

Summary

### 1. Research Question. How can gaze target estimation balance the trade-off between the high annotation costs of complex multi-branch methods and the semantic limitations of streamlined designs? The paper investigates whether Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) can provide the necessary semantic context to improve accuracy without increasing the burden of manual supervision.

### 2. Positioning. The work addresses a critical dichotomy in current gaze estimation research. It fills the gap between data-hungry multi-branch architectures that require extensive annotation and streamlined models that often fail to capture high-level gaze intent by relying too heavily on low-level visual saliency.

### 3. Key Contribution. The paper introduces TextGaze, a unified cross-modal architecture that leverages Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) to provide scalable semantic guidance. It proposes a novel transformer-based fusion module with hierarchical text supervision to align visual features with textual scene cues effectively.

### 4. Methodology. The model uses a frozen visual encoder for feature extraction and an LVLM to generate gaze-aligned textual cues. These modalities are combined in a transformer fusion module, which feeds into lightweight decoding heads to predict gaze heatmaps and determine in/out-of-frame status.

### 5. Limitations. Potential limitations include the computational overhead introduced by the LVLM component compared to purely visual streamlined models. The system also relies on the LVLM's capacity to accurately interpret and describe scene semantics, which may vary across diverse or ambiguous visual domains.

### 6. Critical Evaluation. This work offers a significant advancement by demonstrating how LVLMs can improve domain generalization in gaze estimation. While the addition of a language model increases architectural complexity, the ability to generalize across datasets without fine-tuning makes this a compelling approach for real-world applications.

Predicting Human Visual Attention on Words in Source Code

Authors: Chia-Yi Su, Collin McMillan
arXiv: 2607.14535v1

Summary

1. Research Question
This paper investigates whether a computational model can accurately predict where software engineers look when reading source code. It aims to determine if aligning machine attention with human gaze can improve performance on software engineering tasks.

2. Positioning
The work builds upon existing software engineering research that uses eye-tracking to understand programmer cognition. It fills a gap by moving from simply observing these patterns to creating a predictive model that maps artificial neural network attention directly to human visual attention.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is a novel loss function that computes the similarity between human eye-tracking data and the internal attention mechanisms of an artificial neural network. This approach allows the model to learn from human gaze patterns effectively, resulting in significant performance improvements over existing baselines.

4. Methodology
The authors propose a neural network model optimized with a custom loss function designed to align model attention weights with human fixation data. They evaluate this model on three distinct datasets involving Java and C code, using metrics like Pearson correlation and normalized Levenshtein distance for scanpath prediction.

5. Limitations
The study is limited to only two programming languages, Java and C, which may restrict the generalizability of the findings to other languages or paradigms. Additionally, while the abstract claims the model outperforms GPT-5, it does not detail the specific baselines or the context of that comparison in this summary.

6. Critical Evaluation
This research offers a compelling bridge between cognitive science and deep learning by treating human gaze as a supervisory signal for machine attention. The reported performance gains are substantial, particularly the high correlation improvements, suggesting the model captures human reading behavior well. However, the claim regarding GPT-5 performance is bold and warrants closer examination of the full paper to verify the testing conditions.

Analysis of Mutual and Referential Human and Robot Gazes in a Collaborative Word Association Game

Authors: Jens V. Rüppel, Tim Schreiter, Andrey Rudenko et al.
arXiv: 2607.12181v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates how robot gaze behaviors influence human visual attention during a cognitively demanding collaborative task. It asks whether robot gaze orientation affects where humans look and if humans use gaze to seek confirmation from the robot during interaction.

2. Positioning
Existing research on robot gaze focuses heavily on casual, face-to-face social conversations. This work positions itself in the gap regarding gaze dynamics in task-focused, high-workload scenarios where efficient coordination is critical.

3. Key Contribution
The study provides empirical evidence that in demanding tasks, verbal signals tend to overshadow non-verbal gaze cues from the robot. It also confirms that humans significantly increase their gaze towards the robot when their speech contains confirmation requests, offering specific design insights for robot turn-taking in collaborative settings.

4. Methodology
Participants played a collaborative word association game with a NAO robot powered by a Large Language Model to generate dialogue. The researchers compared two gaze conditions, mutual and referential, and used eye-tracking glasses to measure human fixation times and gaze patterns relative to specific areas of interest.

5. Limitations
The study acknowledges that the specific cognitive load of the word game may limit the generalizability of the results to other types of tasks. Additionally, the physical embodiment of the NAO robot might influence gaze perception differently compared to more human-like or screen-based agents.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work offers a valuable intersection of embodied AI and LLMs, providing a realistic testbed for studying complex interaction dynamics. A key strength is the use of precise eye-tracking metrics to quantify attention, though the finding that verbal cues dominate gaze in high-load tasks suggests that designers must prioritize speech synchronization over complex gaze animations for difficult tasks.

LEXIC: Lightweight Eye-tracking eXtension via Injected Complexity

Authors: Sumin Lee, Kyeonghun Kim, Subeen Lee et al.
arXiv: 2607.08152v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper investigates whether gaze-only models for reading comprehension prediction can be significantly improved without relying on full language model integration. It specifically asks if injecting lightweight, precomputed linguistic complexity signals can bridge the performance gap between gaze-only baselines and text-aware models.

2. Positioning
Current research on the EyeBench benchmark reveals a major performance disparity. Text-aware models utilizing pretrained language models achieve AUROC scores of 56 to 63 percent, while gaze-only models perform essentially at chance levels. This work positions itself as a lightweight alternative, attempting to enhance gaze-only performance by incorporating external linguistic knowledge without the computational overhead of running a live language model.

3. Key Contribution
The authors propose LEXIC, a method to augment gaze-only models with three precomputed word-level difficulty signals. These signals are GPT-2 surprisal, word frequency, and word length. The primary contribution is the demonstration that these simple, injected features can yield statistically significant performance gains on the OneStop reading comprehension task, offering a cost-effective middle ground between pure gaze processing and heavy text-aware modeling.

4. Methodology
The approach builds upon the EyeBench AhnCNN baseline by injecting difficulty signals into the per-fixation input. The paper introduces two specific mechanisms for this injection. LEXIC-Concat directly appends the features to the input vector. LEXIC-Res uses a residual approach where a small head predicts a typical reader's gaze response, and the encoder processes the deviation between the predicted and actual gaze.

5. Limitations
The study identifies a specific architectural boundary in the LEXIC-Res mechanism, which struggles to generalize to Unseen Readers. The authors attribute this to the prediction head being calibrated to the specific reading patterns seen during training, leading to imperfect transfer to out-of-distribution subjects. Additionally, the performance gains, while statistically significant, are modest compared to the gap that still exists between gaze-only and text-aware models.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work provides a practical and computationally efficient method for boosting gaze-only model performance using linguistic priors. The strength of the approach lies in its simplicity and the clear ablation of different injection strategies. However, the reliance on precomputed features like GPT-2 surprisal means the model is not entirely language-model-free, which slightly complicates the "lightweight" claim depending on the availability of these features during inference.

CoMind: Understanding Collaborative Human Activity from Multiple Minds and Views

Authors: Alexey Gavryushin, Dingxi Zhang, Zhao Huang et al.
arXiv: 2607.06691v1

Summary

1. Research Question
The paper addresses the challenge of modeling the cognitive processes, specifically Theory of Mind, that underpin natural human collaboration. It seeks to determine how multimodal data, particularly gaze, can be used to infer mental states and enable AI systems to understand and anticipate collaborative actions.

2. Positioning
Existing research has largely concentrated on modeling physical coordination and task execution mechanics in isolation. This work fills a critical gap by providing a dataset specifically designed to analyze the cognitive and social dimensions of teamwork, moving beyond simple action recognition to the inference of shared mental states.

3. Key Contribution
The primary contribution is the introduction of the CoMind dataset, a rich multimodal collection capturing real-world cooking collaboration through both egocentric and exocentric views. It uniquely integrates gaze tracking with 3D scene scans to facilitate research on shared attention and social reasoning. Additionally, the paper establishes three new benchmarks for Joint Attention Estimation, Socially Conditioned Object Interaction Anticipation, and Collaborative Handover Prediction.

4. Methodology
The authors collected a novel dataset featuring pairs of participants engaged in cooking tasks, equipped with head-mounted cameras and mobile eye-trackers. This visual and gaze data was synchronized with high-quality audio and 3D object scans to create a temporally aligned record of the collaborative process. The data was annotated for specific social cues, shared attention instances, and object interactions to support the proposed benchmarks.

5. Limitations
The dataset is focused exclusively on cooking scenarios, which may limit the generalizability of models trained on it to other collaborative domains like professional office work or manufacturing. The reliance on wearable sensors for gaze tracking, while accurate, may introduce hardware constraints that affect the naturalness of the interaction. Furthermore, the complexity of fusing egocentric and exocentric views poses significant technical challenges for real-time processing.

6. Critical Evaluation
This work represents a significant step forward for embodied AI, particularly in its use of gaze as a proxy for modeling Theory of Mind. The explicit focus on shared attention and anticipation provides a necessary bridge between low-level perception and high-level social reasoning. However, the effectiveness of this dataset will ultimately depend on the ability of future models to handle the inherent noise and occlusion common in real-world multi-camera setups.
